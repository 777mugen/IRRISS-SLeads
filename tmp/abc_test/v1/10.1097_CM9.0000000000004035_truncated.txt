Abstract Plain Language Summary

* * *

The tumor immune microenvironment (TIME) plays a pivotal role in tumor initiation, progression, and therapeutic response, and is closely associated with long-term treatment efficacy. Notably, therapeutic interventions are not only influenced by the immune microenvironment but also actively remodel its cellular and molecular architecture. In recent years, advanced technologies such as single-cell sequencing, spatial transcriptomics, and other multi-omics approaches have provided unprecedented insights into the dynamic interplay between therapeutic modalities and the TIME, as well as the secondary microenvironmental changes that critically influence immunotherapy outcomes. In this review, we summarize therapy-induced remodeling of the TIME, elucidate the mechanisms by which these changes modulate immunotherapy responsiveness, and discuss potential strategies for therapeutic optimization. A deeper understanding of therapy-driven alterations in the TIME, empowered by emerging high-resolution technologies, will not only facilitate the monitoring and prediction of treatment efficacy but also guide the development of more precise and individualized combination treatment strategies.

Introduction
------------

Tumorigenesis is an evolutionary process influenced not just by the accumulation of genetic and epigenetic alterations within malignant cells, but also by their reciprocal interactions with a permissive tumor microenvironment (TME).[[1–3](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] This dynamic ecosystem orchestrates every stage of cancer progression—from immune evasion to metastatic dissemination—and profoundly influences therapeutic responses and relapse patterns with immune cells assuming dual and often paradoxical roles: They can eliminate transformed cells but also facilitate tumor persistence and resistance due to chronic stimulation and metabolic reprogramming.[[4–6](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Recent studies have revealed that cancer therapies do not merely act on tumor cells but actively remodel the immune landscape of the TME, which can not only reinvigorate antitumor immunity but may also promote adaptive resistance, which undermines long-term efficacy.[[7,8](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Therefore, deciphering the relevant underlying mechanisms is central to developing next-generation cancer immunotherapies.

Immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) restores T-cell activity and yields durable responses in subsets of patients, indicating the transformative potential of TME-focused interventions.[[9](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] However, the objective response rate of anti-programmed cell death (PD)-1/programmed cell death ligand 1 (PD-L1) therapy remains <20% across most tumor types.[[10–12](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Beyond T cells, advanced single-cell and spatial multi-omics analyses have uncovered striking inter- and intratumoral heterogeneity in immune infiltration, with myeloid populations exhibiting remarkable therapy-induced plasticity.[[13](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] For instance, radiotherapy can enhance antigen release yet simultaneously recruit immunosuppressive macrophages; chemotherapy may debulk tumors but drive myeloid-derived suppressor cell (MDSC) expansion; targeted therapies can reshape stromal and vascular compartments; and ICB itself can induce compensatory pathways of resistance, such as interferon-γ (IFN-γ)-driven immunoediting.[[14–16](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Thus, therapeutic interventions not only target cancer cells but also dynamically reprogram the immune milieu, which in turn dictates treatment outcomes.

Despite major advances in radiotherapy, chemotherapy, targeted therapy, and immunotherapy, substantial challenges remain in expanding the responder population, improving durability, and minimizing toxicity. Crucially, the TME is not a static entity, but adapts under therapeutic pressure, establishing a bidirectional regulatory relationship with malignant cells.[[17](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Understanding these therapy-driven immune changes, including cytokine signaling, metabolic rewiring, stromal remodeling, and immune cell plasticity, is essential for elucidating the mechanisms underlying tolerance and resistance.[[18–20](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Integrating these mechanistic insights with development of biomarkers and rational combination therapies is pivotal for aligning therapy-induced immune remodeling with enhanced and durable antitumor immunity.[[21–23](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In this review, we synthesize the current understanding of how diverse cancer therapies reshape the TME, delineate the molecular and cellular mechanisms underpinning therapy-induced immune plasticity, and discuss strategies to improve the precision and efficacy of modern immunotherapy.

Impact of Cancer Therapy on TME Remodeling
------------------------------------------

Tumors develop from the dynamic interplay between malignant cells and their surrounding stromal niches.[[2–4,24](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Among the stromal components, immune cells have a critical role in influencing tumor progression/regression and patient responses to diverse therapeutic modalities.[[18,20,25](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Treatment induces significant heterogeneous remodeling of the tumor immune microenvironment (TIME), which is a major determinant of variable responses to the same therapy.[[19,25](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Recent clinical and translational studies have identified which TIME changes are positively or negatively correlated with therapeutic outcomes: Beneficial remodeling, including robust infiltration of effector CD8+ T cells, and formation of tertiary lymphoid structures (TLS), strongly correlate with improved responses to ICB (supported by clinical trial evidence (NCT03651271, NCT06241066, and NCT06883565))[[17,21](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)]; conversely, deleterious alterations, such as therapy-driven accumulation of immunosuppressive M2-like macrophages, monocytic myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), and regulatory T cells (Tregs), are consistently associated with treatment resistance and poor outcomes across ICB therapy, chemotherapy, and targeted therapies.[[18,22](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Thus, TIME remodeling is a decisive factor that can either empower antitumor immunity or reinforce an immunosuppressive niche that drives therapeutic failure and disease progression.[[7–9,26](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] A thorough understanding of these dynamics is therefore essential for deciphering the mechanistic basis of therapeutic response and designing rational precision interventions and combinatorial strategies to maximize clinical benefits.

### Tumor-infiltrating T cells

Long-term success of cancer therapy depends on the capacity of the immune system to recognize and eliminate malignant cells.[[17–19,24,27](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Within adaptive immune cells, T lymphocytes represent the dominant effector population orchestrating tumor rejection. Cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) exert antitumor functions through perforin, granzymes, and death-inducing ligands such as FasL, while simultaneously modulating the tumor immune landscape via cytokines including IFN-γ.[[28](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] ICB, particularly antibodies targeting PD-1/PD-L1, has revolutionized cancer therapy by reinvigorating exhausted T cells and restoring their effector functions. Activated CD8+ T cells secrete IFN-γ and enhance antigen presentation via upregulating major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules, inducing antigen-presenting cells (APCs), and recruiting T cells and natural killer (NK) cells through T helper 1 (Th1)-type chemokines.[[29](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] These feedforward loops constitute the immunological foundation of the efficacy of ICB. IFN-γ promotes tumor ferroptosis by downregulating solute carrier family 3 member 2 (SLC3A2) and solute carrier family 7 member 11 (SLC7A11), suggesting synergistic interactions between ferroptotic death and antitumor immunity.[[30,31](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Nonetheless, chronic IFN-γ signaling also activates compensatory immunosuppressive circuits, including PD-L1, indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO), arginase, and serpin peptidase inhibitor, clade B, member 9 (SERPINB9), which can impair T cell function, promote therapeutic resistance, and sometimes drive hyperprogressive disease [[Figure 1](javascript:void(0))].[[32,33](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] This duality of IFN-γ—simultaneously activating and suppressing immunity—provides a mechanistic explanation for the limited response rate to ICB (20–30%) and highlights the need to elucidate IFN-γ-driven feedback networks to optimize therapeutic efficacy.[[34](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] To support this, melanoma and microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) colorectal cancer (CRC), which harbor abundant neoantigens and maintain intact Janus kinase/signal transducer and activator of transcription (JAK/STAT)–MHC signaling, typically remain in a state where IFN-γ-driven antigen presentation and ferroptotic sensitization dominate over adaptive immune resistance.[[26](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Conversely, hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) and microsatellite stable (MSS) CRC exhibit a lower threshold for IFN-γ-induced immunosuppression due to metabolic constraints, stromal exclusion, and enrichment of tolerogenic myeloid cells, resulting in dominant PD-L1, IDO1, and exhaustion signatures.[[29,34](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Lung cancers further illustrate this spectrum: Smoking-related non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) is associated with partial IFN-γ responsiveness, whereas small cell lung cancer (SCLC) rapidly engages immunosuppressive signaling despite its high tumor mutational burden (TMB).[[35](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] These tumor-type-specific IFN-γ response patterns provide a mechanistic framework for understanding why some cancers respond to ICB whereas others develop primary or adaptive resistance.

Conventional cytotoxic strategies also profoundly modulate T cell immunity within the TME. Radiotherapy and chemotherapy not only induce immunogenic tumor cell death and enhance MHC-I expression, thereby increasing CTL recognition, but also directly modulate the immune landscape.[[7](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Chemotherapeutic agents such as doxorubicin and gemcitabine selectively deplete immunosuppressive subsets, including polymorphonuclear myeloid-derived suppressor cells (PMN-MDSCs) and Tregs,[[36,37](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] and relieve the inhibitory pressure on effector T cells; 5-fluorouracil, oxaliplatin, and pemetrexed, promote T-cell infiltration and improve effector function, thereby synergizing with ICB to achieve superior responses.[[38–40](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] These observations challenge the traditional view of cytotoxic therapies as purely tumoricidal and position them as strategic partners in immunotherapy-centric regimens.

In addition to CTLs, CD4+ Th cells are critical for orchestrating antitumor immunity. Th cells reinforce CTL activity by enhancing antigen presentation, stimulating B cell responses, and restraining immunoregulatory populations. For instance, in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma, combined PD-L1 and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 (CTLA-4) blockade drives Th1 polarization, augments APC function within draining lymph nodes, and enhances CD8+ T cell activation, correlating with pathological regression.[[41](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Emerging evidence also highlights the potential of Th9 cells, which simultaneously activate dendritic cells (DCs), CTLs, and NK cells to enhance immune infiltration and tumor clearance in melanoma, lung, and breast cancer models.[[42–44](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Conversely, Th17 and Th22 cells are implicated in promoting immune tolerance and tumor progression in specific contexts.[[45–47](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Moreover, follicular regulatory T cell (Tfr, CD4+CXCR5+GITR+), recently identified in NSCLC, melanoma, and CRC, suppress germinal center B cell responses and restrict antigen-specific T cell expansion, thereby acting as a negative biomarker of immunotherapy response [[Figure 1](javascript:void(0))].[[48](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Thus, the balance of Th subsets critically determines therapeutic outcomes. Advances in single-cell sequencing and spatial transcriptomics now allow precise mapping of T cell heterogeneity within the TME,[[49–51](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] and facilitate response prediction and design of refined immunotherapeutic strategies.

### Tumor-infiltrating B Cells

Additionally, B cells are pivotal components of the adaptive immune system. They mediate tumor elimination through the secretion of immunoglobulin G (IgG) antibodies that drive antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) and opsonization, and also act as APCs that activate effector T cells, reinforcing antitumor immunity.[[52](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Anticancer therapies profoundly alter the composition and function of tumor-infiltrating B cell subsets; for example, gemcitabine–cisplatin chemotherapy in nasopharyngeal carcinoma induces innate-like CD27+IgD+IgM+ B cells,[[53](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] whereas neoadjuvant chemotherapy in breast cancer promotes the inducible T-cell costimulator ligand (ICOSL)+complete receptor (CR)2 high IL-10–CD20+CD38+CD27+IgA–IgD– subset.[[54](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Despite phenotypic diversity across cancer types, these therapy-induced B cells consistently promote TLS formation and enhance CD8+ T cell responses via the ICOSL–inducible costimulator (ICOS) axis, thereby contributing to persistent antitumor immunity. Consequently, their abundance may serve as a predictive biomarker of chemotherapy responsiveness, providing a rationale for integrating B cell-oriented strategies into combinatorial chemo-immunotherapy.[[53,55,56](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)]

However, certain B cell populations acquire immunosuppressive programs that attenuate therapeutic efficacy. In pancreatic cancer, stimulator of interferon genes (STING) agonists promote B cells to become IL-35+ regulatory B cells (Bregs), which suppress NK cell proliferation and promote immune escape, whereas ablation of B cell-intrinsic STING signaling enhances tumor control.[[57](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In glioma, agonistic CD40 antibodies (αCD40) facilitate TLS development but also induce CD11b+ B cells that impair T cell function and limit responses to checkpoint blockade.[[58](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Beyond Bregs, plasma cells can also induce tumor progression; in HCC, epigenetic reprogramming therapy increases T cell infiltration while concurrently driving IgG+ plasma cell differentiation, which polarize macrophages toward a protumor phenotype and decrease immunotherapy efficacy [[Figure 1](javascript:void(0))].[[59,60](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Collectively, these studies highlight the divergent plasticity of therapy-modulated B cells, which can either reinforce protective immunity or establish immunosuppressive circuits. Understanding the molecular programs that determine the fate of B cells within the TME is critical for optimizing existing therapies and uncovering novel therapeutic targets.

### NK cells

NK cells, the third lymphocyte subset, play a central role in antitumor immunity. By activating receptors such as natural killer group 2D (NKG2D) and natural killer cell p46-related protein (NKp46), NK cells can detect stressed or transformed cells and eliminate them via direct cytotoxicity, cytokine secretion, and ADCC. Similar to T cells, NK cell activity is tightly controlled by multiple inhibitory receptors such as natural killer group 2A (NKG2A), lymphocyte activation gene 3 (LAG-3), T-cell immunoglobulin and mucin-domain containing-3 (TIM-3), and T-cell immunoglobulin and ITIM domain protein (TIGIT).[[61](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Clinical evidence indicates that blocking these checkpoints can reinvigorate NK cell function, underscoring their post-treatment therapeutic plasticity.[[62](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Following therapy, NK cells are highly sensitive to the remodeled TME, with substantially alterated infiltration, phenotype, and effector functions.[[63](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Certain radiotherapy and chemotherapy regimens induce immunogenic cell death and release chemotactic signals that enhance NK cell recruitment,[[61,64](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] whereas immunosuppressive factors, such as transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β), metabolic rewiring, and hypoxia can restrict NK cell infiltration, survival, and functionality.[[65,66](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)]

In fact, post-treatment intratumoral NK cells often adopt tissue-resident or hypofunctional states, marked by upregulated inhibitory receptors, diminished cytotoxic granule release, and reduced IFN-γ production [[Figure 1](javascript:void(0))].[[67,68](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Crucially, these dysfunctional states are reversible; immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), cytokine stimulation (IL-15 and IL-21), or combination immunotherapies can restore NK effector activity.[[69–71](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Consequently, NK cells are increasingly recognized as pivotal targets for immune reconstitution after cancer therapy.[[72](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Clinically, the adoptively transferred allogeneic NK cells and _ex vivo_-expanded, cryopreserved NK cell products showed promising antitumor activity. Emerging strategies—including feeder cell-based expansion systems expressing membrane-bound IL-15 or IL-21, genetic engineering techniques to overcome TGF-β mediated suppression and hypoxia, and chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-NK cell therapies—have promising therapeutic potential.[[61,73](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Early-phase clinical trials (phase I/II) have reported promising efficacy, and combinatorial strategies with radiotherapy, chemotherapy, or checkpoint blockade are under active investigation.[[74](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Thus, a detailed mechanistic understanding of therapy-induced NK cell remodeling, coupled with technologies that enhance persistence, pharmacokinetics, and resistance to immunosuppression, is essential for translating NK-based therapies into stable clinical benefits.

### DCs

DCs are professional APCs, comprising conventional DC subsets (cDC1 and cDC2), plasmacytoid DCs, and monocyte-derived DCs. Within the TME, DCs are critical for initiating and coordinating anti-tumor T cell responses.[[75](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Conventional cancer therapies, including radiotherapy and chemotherapy, not only induce tumor cell death, but also release tumor-associated antigens and damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), such as calreticulin (CRT), high-mobility group box 1 (HMGB1), and adenosine triphosphate (ATP). In draining lymph nodes, these signals enhance antigen uptake and presentation by DCs, thereby priming CTLs and reinforcing antitumor immunity.[[7](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] However, immunosuppressive elements in TME, including Tregs, limit DC function.[[76](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Some chemotherapeutics also reduce immunosuppressive populations, including Tregs and PMN-MDSCs, indirectly boosting DC-mediated immune responses.[[8,77](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Beyond conventional therapies, ICB reshapes DC function; cDC1 not only cross-presents antigens via MHC-I to prime CD8+ T cells but also engages MHC-II to activate CD4+ T cells, orchestrating coordinated T cell responses.[[78](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In HCC, spatial interactions between PD-1+ T cell factor 1 (TCF-1)+ CD8+ progenitor exhausted T cells (Tpex) and C-X-C motif chemokine ligand (CXCL)13+PD-1+ CD4+ T cells occur via DC clusters enriched with maturation and immunoregulatory molecules, promoting Tpex differentiation into effector T cells and enhancing antitumor immunity.[[79](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Thus, effective responses to ICB rely not only on the abundance or activation state of cDCs and T cells, but also on their appropriate spatial organization within the TME, which enables coordinated CD4+ and CD8+ T cell activation.

Paradoxically, high intratumoral cDC1 densities were observed in checkpoint blockade-resistant patients, indicating altered cDC functionality.[[79](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Single-cell RNA sequencing of human and murine NSCLC identified cDC1 and cDC2 clusters co-expressing high levels of immunoregulatory molecules (CD274/PD-L1, PDCD1LG2, CD200) and maturation genes (_Cd40_, _Ccr7_, _Il12b_), a subset termed tumor-infiltrating mature regulatory DCs (mregDCs) [[Figure 1](javascript:void(0))].[[80](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] These mregDCs likely arise from both cDC1 and cDC2 and correspond to previously described DC3 or lysosomal-associated membrane protein 3 (LAMP3)+ DCs. They differ from other DC subsets by the coordinated expression of maturation markers (LAMP3, CD80, and CD83), migratory molecules (CCR7), and chemokines (chemokine [C-C motif] ligand [CCL]19 and CCL21).[[80,81](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Functionally, mregDCs migrate to tumor-draining lymph nodes to prime T cells, while maintaining immunosuppression via high PD-L1 expression.[[78](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Their differentiation is regulated by AXL signaling, which promotes PD-L1; and by IL-4, which suppresses IL-12. IL-4 blockade restores IL-12 secretion by cDC1 and enhances antitumor T-cell responses, thereby reducing ICB resistance.[[78](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Collectively, these insights highlight that targeted modulation of DC function may improve therapeutic efficacy and overcome resistance to immunotherapy.

### Tumor-infiltrating macrophages

Macrophages, pivotal orchestrators bridging innate and adaptive immunity, undergo extensive phenotypic and functional reprogramming following anti-cancer therapies, thereby exerting a dual protective and deleterious influence on therapeutic outcomes. Macrophages can be reprogrammed into potent antitumor effectors. For example, radiotherapy drives M1 polarization via extracellular vesicle signaling, thereby enhancing T-cell priming and amplifying therapeutic efficacy.[[7,82](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Trastuzumab enhances Fcγ receptor-mediated antibody-dependent cellular phagocytosis (ADCP) by macrophages,[[83](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] whereas the mouse double minute 2 (MDM2) inhibitor alrizomadlin not only activates p53 signaling in tumor cells but also restrains M2 differentiation, curbing the establishment of immunosuppressive niches.[[84](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Similarly, neoadjuvant immunotherapy reshapes macrophage dynamics by expanding CD16+ CX3C chemokine receptor 1 (CX3CR1)+ “patrolling” monocytes that strengthen immune surveillance while reducing secreted phosphoprotein 1 (SPP1)+ macrophages associated with PD-L1+IDO1+CCL3+ senescent granulocytes.[[85](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Therapy-induced immunogenic cell death further reinforces M1 polarization by suppressing triggering receptor expressed on myeloid cells (TREM) 2 and promoting inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS)-dependent vascular normalization, thereby creating a positive immune feedback loop.[[86](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Clinically, tiragolumab (anti-TIGIT) can trigger Fc-dependent activation of macrophages and monocytes within the TME, enhancing atezolizumab (anti-PD-L1) efficacy in lung cancer by reprogramming CTLs from an exhausted to a memory-like state.[[87](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Together, these findings underscore the therapeutic plasticity of macrophages as reprogrammable effectors of antitumor immunity.

Conversely, macrophages can sustain immunosuppression and therapeutic resistance, which compromises their clinical benefits. Local interventions such as radiotherapy or thermal ablation release DAMPs that recruit tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), fuel maladaptive inflammation, suppress T cell activity, and promote metastasis.[[7,88](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In HCC, sorafenib induces oxidized lipid accumulation and Toll-like receptor (TLR) ligand release, thereby activating inflammasome pathways in macrophages that paradoxically promote angiogenesis and therapy resistance.[[89,90](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Similarly, ICB-induced IFN-γ signaling from activated T cells enhances sialylation of plasma cell-derived IgG, which engages DC-specific intercellular adhesion molecule (ICAM)-3 grabbing non-integrin (SIGN)+ macrophages to activate Raf-1/activating transcription factor 3 (ATF3) signaling, thereby suppressing cyclic GMP-AMP synthase (cGAS)-STING and blunting type I IFN-dependent immunity [[Figure 1](javascript:void(0))].[[60](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Clinical observations also reveal macrophage-driven tolerance, such as FasL-dependent clearance of T cells by liver-resident macrophages in patients with hepatic metastases, or Tim4+ peritoneal macrophages suppressing CTL expansion and promoting peritoneal dissemination.[[91,92](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In MSI-H CRC, ICB drives the reorganization of the microenvironment in non-pathological complete response (pCR) tumors, characterized by expansion of IL-1β+ monocytes/macrophages and CCL2+ fibroblasts alongside depletion of major histocompatibility complex, class II, DR alpha (HLA-DRA)+ endothelial cells, CXCL12+ fibroblasts, CD4+ T cells, and CD20+ B cells, thereby impairing effector CD8+ T cell expansion and favoring exhaustion.[[93](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Thus, the post-therapeutic plasticity of macrophages determines the outcome, representing both a tractable therapeutic ally and a potential Achilles heel through which tolerance and resistance arise.

### Tumor-infiltrating neutrophils

Neutrophils, long regarded as first-line effectors of innate immunity, have significant functional diversity within the TME following therapy. Therapy-associated neutrophils can amplify local inflammation and promote tumor progression by driving angiogenesis, facilitating epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT), and enhancing resistance to cytotoxic agents.[[94](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] For example, anthracycline-based chemotherapy (such as doxorubicin) triggers neutrophil extracellular trap (NETs) formation, which releases chromatin and proteolytic enzymes into the local milieu, exacerbating myocardial inflammation and contributing to cardiotoxicity [[Figure 1](javascript:void(0))].[[95](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In HCC, elevated neutrophil infiltration correlates with reduced survival and poor responsiveness, underscoring their negative prognostic value for advanced disease.[[96](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Therefore, tumor–immune crosstalk during therapy not only reshapes the immune architecture, but can paradoxically reinforce malignant behavior, ultimately compromising therapeutic efficacy.

Encouragingly, not all therapy-associated neutrophils exert protumorigenic effects. Certain subsets exert antitumor effector functions by phagocytosing and killing malignant cells, thereby amplifying T cell responses and promoting tumor regression.[[97](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] A pan-cancer single-cell transcriptomic atlas across 17 tumor types identified HLA-DR+CD74+ neutrophils with antigen-presenting capacity as robust predictors of immunotherapy responsiveness.[[98](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In CRC and melanoma, triple immunotherapy induces the recruitment and activation of iNOS-expressing neutrophils by T cells, which eliminates antigen-loss tumor variants and reinforces durable therapeutic efficacy.[[99](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Therefore, post-therapeutic selective reprogramming or targeting of discrete neutrophil subsets can potentially restore effective antitumor immunity and improve clinical outcomes.

### MDSCs

MDSCs, encompassing PMN-MDSC and monocytic (M-MDSC) lineages, represent a heterogeneous population of immature myeloid cells endowed with potent immunosuppressive programs that critically shape the post-therapy tumor immune landscape.[[100](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Diverse anticancer interventions, including immunotherapy, radiotherapy, chemotherapy, and targeted therapies, activate the tumor and stromal compartments to secrete chemokines (such as, CCL2 and CXCL family members), alarmins such as S100A8/A9, and growth factors including granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) and vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), thereby orchestrating MDSCs recruitment and expansion. These cues converge on signal transducer and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3) and CCAAT/enhancer-binding protein (C/EBP)β signaling to maintain the cells in an immature and suppressive state.[[101](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Once recruited into the TME, MDSCs attenuate CTL and NK cell function through arginase-1, iNOS, and reactive oxygen species (ROS), while indirectly promoting Treg and Breg differentiation via IL-10 and TGF-β. Beyond immune suppression, MDSCs promote angiogenesis, extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling, and EMT, thereby reinforcing a protumorigenic milieu that supports disease persistence and metastatic dissemination.[[101](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Mechanistic insights from HCC have revealed that radiofrequency ablation (RFA) upregulates mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue lymphoma translocation protein 1 (METTL1)-dependent tumor-derived TGF-β2, which in turn drives compensatory PMN-MDSC infiltration, blunting the therapeutic efficacy of RFA and exemplifying MDSCs as active mediators of post-treatment immune tolerance.[[102](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Further, radiotherapy-induced MDSC accumulation and PD-L1 upregulation at distant sites are linked to enhanced metastatic potential[[103](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)]; in oxaliplatin-resistant HCC, a discoidin domain receptor tyrosine kinase 2 (DDR2)/STAT3 feedback circuit promotes immune escape by increasing PD-L1 expression and recruiting MDSCs,[[104](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] while tumor-derived exosomal PD-L1 expands MDSCs and accelerates gastric cancer progression.[[105](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] These findings suggest that MDSCs are not passive bystanders but dynamic architects of therapy-induced immune suppression. Understanding and disruting the molecular circuits that govern MDSC recruitment, differentiation, and suppressive function, could unlock new avenues for stable therapeutic reprogramming and improved clinical outcomes.[[106](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)]

### Tumor stromal cells

The tumor stroma forms an essential and highly dynamic compartment of the TME. Beyond immune elements, marked heterogeneity exists among fibroblast and endothelial cell (EC) populations, which collectively modulate immune dynamics.[[1–3,25](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Integrative machine learning frameworks combining histopathological and transcriptomic datasets have revealed the spatial and functional complexity of stromal–immune crosstalk,[[19–21](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] indicating that non-immune stromal elements can critically influence both immune cell states and responses to ICB.

#### ECs

ECs lining the luminal surface of blood vessels constitute the structural and functional foundation of the vascular niche and serve as key orchestrators of tumor angiogenesis. Compared to physiological vessels, tumor-associated vasculature is abnormal, structurally disorganized, hyperpermeable, and characterized by weakened junctional integrity[[107](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)]; this not only facilitates tumor invasion and dissemination but also restricts effector immune cell trafficking, thereby inducing immunosuppression and limiting the efficacy of multiple therapies.[[107](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Following treatment, dynamic alterations in vascular architecture and function have emerged as pivotal determinants of immune reprogramming and therapeutic outcomes. Anti-angiogenic therapy, beyond depriving tumors of nutrient supply, can induce “vascular normalization”, reinstate vessel functionality, and improve immune infiltration and drug perfusion. Clinically, combining antiangiogenic agents with ICB produces synergistic benefits.[[108](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] For instance, in HCC, anti-PD-1 therapy combined with lenvatinib enhances effector/memory CD8+ T-cell infiltration and reduces Tregs and exhausted CD8+ T-cell frequencies, correlating with improved response rates.[[109](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Mechanistically, VEGF signaling not only drives neovascularization but also imposes the following immunoregulatory effects: impairs DC maturation, induces endothelial FasL-dependent CD8+ T-cell apoptosis, downregulates chemokines (CXCL10, CXCL11) and adhesion molecules (ICAM-1, VCAM-1), and promotes Treg and MDSC recruitment.[[110](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Targeting VEGF offers the dual benefit of vascular normalization and immune reactivation. Bispecific antibodies that co-target the PD-1/PD-L1 and VEGF pathways are under active clinical evaluation and hold promise for augmenting durable post-therapy immune control.

#### Cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs)

CAFs, the core architects of the tumor stroma, are crucial in orchestrating TME remodeling following therapy. Chemotherapeutic agents such as doxorubicin induce fibroblast senescence, which in turn promotes NET formation and reawakens dormant tumor cells, whereas co-treatment with senolytics (such as dasatinib and quercetin) counteracts this effect and suppresses metastatic relapse [[Figure 1](javascript:void(0))].[[111](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Radiotherapy and targeted therapies remodel CAF phenotypes, alter the immune infiltration landscape, and modulate therapeutic outcomes. Paclitaxel-based chemotherapy, while being directly cytotoxic to tumor cells, concurrently injures fibroblasts and triggers IL-6 and granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF) release, which activates mitogen-activated protein kinase kinase 1/2 (MEK1/2) signaling and reactivates dormant tumor populations.[[76,112,113](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In addition to paracrine signaling, CAFs remodel the ECM to form physical and biochemical barriers that impede immune cell entry while secreting metabolites and cytokines that promote hypoxia, lactate accumulation, and recruitment of Tregs and MDSCs, thereby attenuating immunotherapy efficacy.[[1,114,115](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Furthermore, mechanical stress within the stroma can engage the Piezo1–Osr2 axis, inducing T-cell exhaustion and compromising checkpoint blockade efficacy.[[116](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Accordingly, targeting CAF-driven pathways using anti-fibrotic agents or TGF-β inhibitors in combination with immunotherapy can potentially restore immune infiltration preclinically and clinically and enhance treatment response.[[117](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Thus, CAF plasticity is a central determinant of post-treatment TME evolution and is a promising frontier for therapeutic innovation.

Therapy-induced alterations in the TIME have important implications for clinical management and therapeutic optimization. Post-treatment TIME monitoring provides a real-time window into patient responses, allowing early prediction of therapeutic efficacy and identification of individuals who may benefit from continued or adjusted therapy. Moreover, treatment-induced remodeling can reveal adaptive immunosuppressive feedback mechanisms, including the expansion of M2-like macrophages, Treg cells, and MDSC, which may limit therapeutic benefits.[[88,100–105](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Recognizing these negative feedback loops enables the rational design of combination or sequential treatment strategies, potentially enhancing tumor eradication and overcoming resistance. Therefore, integrating dynamic TIME monitoring into clinical practice can provide precise interventions and support the development of novel combinatorial immunotherapies, increasing the translational relevance of immune microenvironment studies.

Mechanisms by Which the Secondary TME Shapes the Efficacy of Immunotherapy
--------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Modulation of tumor cell features by the secondary microenvironment

#### Loss of tumor antigen expression

The ultimate effect of TME on therapy is manifested in the vulnerability of tumor cells to immune-mediated cytotoxicity. While therapies that activate T cells commonly augment IFN-γ-driven MHC-I expression and promote tumor elimination,[[118](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] selective pressure frequently drives secondary immune escape via antigen loss. Among the key drivers, the secondary microenvironment promotes plastic antigen loss via the inflammatory and autophagy-dependent pathways. Inflammatory cues accelerate autophagy-mediated MHC-I degradation,[[119](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] and loss of the autophagy receptor optineurin further promotes lysosomal degradation of the IFN-γ receptor, thereby attenuating both IFN-γ signaling and MHC-I expression [[Figure 2](javascript:void(0))].[[120](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In preclinical and clinical settings, these defects establish a direct mechanistic link between inflammatory microenvironments and impaired antigen presentation.

Critically, such microenvironmental forces seldom act in isolation, but synergize with tumor-intrinsic alterations to reinforce antigen loss. In paired biopsy analyses of NSCLC, multiple neoantigens disappeared from tumors that acquired resistance to the PD-1 blockade.[[121,122](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Genetic lesions such as JAK1/2 loss-of-function abrogate IFN-γ responsiveness, while β2-microglobulin mutations disrupt MHC I integrity and prevent CTL recognition.[[123](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Moreover, tumor suppressor alterations (in _PTEN, STK11, KEAP1_) reprogram the TME toward immunosuppression and activate oncogenic pathways (mitogen-activated protein kinase, Wingless/Int-1/β-catenin) that further suppress antigen presentation.[[17](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Epigenetic reprogramming compounds these effects: enhancer of _Zeste_ homolog 2 (EZH2)-mediated histone H3 lysine 27 trimethylation (H3K27me3) silences CXCL9/10,[[124](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] AT-rich interaction domain 1A (ARID1A) deficiency blunts IFN-γ responses,[[125](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] and dysregulated DNA methylation by DNA methyltransferase 1 (DNMT1) or ten-eleven translocation 2 (TET2) represses IFN-γ-responsive genes signatures [[Figure 2](javascript:void(0))].[[124](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Thus, secondary microenvironmental cues and intrinsic tumor alterations converge to drive acquired resistance through antigen loss.

Looking ahead, it is imperative to chart the frequency, kinetics, and plasticity of antigen loss across tumor types, and to dissect how microenvironmental signals intersect with genetic and epigenetic programs to mediate immune escape. Therapeutic strategies such as neoantigen vaccines, autophagy inhibitors, or MHC stabilizers show promise, but their clinical translation may be constrained by intratumoral heterogeneity, immune adaptation, and safety margins. Leveraging single-cell and spatial multiomics is essential for resolving spatiotemporal antigen loss dynamics and guiding precision-based interventions. Ultimately, circumventing secondary microenvironment-driven antigen loss requires combination regimens that target both tumor-intrinsic vulnerabilities and the evolving TME niche.

#### Oncogenic and epigenetic interplay with therapy-induced microenvironment

Apart from direct cytotoxic effects, the therapy-induced secondary microenvironment imposes considerable reprogramming pressure on tumor progression and response.[[39,75,117,126](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Chemotherapy depletes tumor cells, liberates antigenic cargo for APC uptake, and rewires local immune and stromal networks. In HCC, for example, chemotherapy fosters crosstalk among central memory-like CD4+ T cells, folate receptor beta (FOLR2)+ macrophages, and matrix metalloproteinase 2 (MMP2)+ fibroblasts, mediated by CXCL12-driven recruitment, which supports TLS formation.[[56](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] However, these favorable immune changes may be accompanied by adversarial molecular modifications: oxaliplatin-based chemotherapy upregulates epigenetic regulator protein arginine methyltransferase 3 (PRMT3) in tumor cells.[[127](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] PRMT3 mediates arginine methylation of heat shock protein (HSP) 60, thereby maintaining mitochondrial homeostasis and restricting the release of mitochondrial DNA, which ultimately dampens the cGAS-STING pathway and suppresses antitumor immunity.[[128](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In ICB, IFN-γ-stimulated macrophages may secrete IL-1β that induces the oncogene proviral integration site for Moloney murine leukemia virus 2 (_PIM2_) in tumor cells, enhancing resistance to T cell-mediated killing and promoting metastasis.[[129](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Thus, not just the microenvironment, but its interplay with intrinsic tumor characteristics, such as oncogenic signaling and epigenetic regulation, dictates the overall response to therapy. Similarly, when IFN-γ signals impinge on tumor stem cells with high aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AHR) expression, the IDO–kynurenine–AHR–p27 axis can drive the dormancy of these stem-like cells.[[130](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] These dormant cells survive therapy and may resurge under permissive conditions, driving relapse.[[131](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Collectively, the secondary microenvironment does not function in isolation, but actively shapes post-treatment adaptation through coordinated interactions among inflammatory cytokines, metabolic circuits, oncogenic drivers, and epigenetic programs. In tumors with distinct genetic backgrounds, an effective therapeutic design must integrate both primary and secondary microenvironmental features with tumor-intrinsic molecular landscapes.

#### Tumor metabolic reprogramming

The secondary TME profoundly reprograms tumor and immune cell metabolism, thereby shaping antigen presentation, T cell functionality, and ultimately, the efficacy of ICB.[[131–134](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Metabolic rewiring under therapeutic pressure acts as a central determinant of immune escape, integrating inflammatory, hypoxic, and nutrient-deprivation cues into coordinated immunoregulatory outcomes. IFN-γ serves as a key metabolic and immunologic orchestrator, regulating MHC-I expression, antigen presentation, T cell activation, and ferroptotic susceptibility.[[30,31,135](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Beyond genetic mechanisms such as canonical _JAK1/2_ mutations, sustained IFN-γ exposure paradoxically reinforces immune tolerance and tumor stemness by rewiring glycolysis (via pyruvate kinase M2 [PKM2] activation), depleting nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+) pools, and stimulating β-catenin, fibroblast growth factor (FGF)2, and cellular myelocytomatosis oncogene (c-Myc) pathways.[[136](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Lactate accumulation and ensuing acidification of the tumor milieu further consolidate immune suppression. Lactate impairs T cell mitochondrial metabolism and viability, drives Treg expansion, and attenuates CTL effector function.[[137](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Simultaneously, therapy-induced hypoxia and oncometabolites, such as succinate and ammonia, amplify these suppressive effects by restricting tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle flux, enforcing epigenetic silencing programs, and precipitating metabolic exhaustion of effector lymphocytes.[[138,139](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Competition for essential amino acids, including methionine and arginine, further compromises CD8+ T cell oxidative metabolism and cytolytic potential,[[140–142](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] whereas dysregulated lipid handling, particularly in the cholesterol and fatty acid pathways, drives T cell exhaustion and ferroptotic death [[Figure 2](javascript:void(0))].[[143–146](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Thus, metabolic reprogramming is a pivotal conduit through which the secondary TME enforces immune evasion and therapeutic resistance.

Targeting metabolic dependencies is a promising strategy for overcoming ICB resistance. Pharmacological interventions such as autophagy inhibitors, lactate transport blockers, and amino acid or lipid metabolism modulators can reinvigorate effector T cell function and restore antitumor immunity.[[23,133–143](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Moreover, anti-angiogenic therapy alleviates hypoxia and enhances immune infiltration through vascular normalization,[[147](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] while host systemic metabolic states, such as adherence to a high-fiber or Mediterranean diet, correlate with superior ICB responsiveness.[[148](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Interestingly, although obesity accelerates immune aging and PD-1-mediated dysfunction through leptin signaling, both preclinical and clinical studies paradoxically associate obesity with enhanced ICB efficacy.[[149](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] The integration of single-cell metabolomics and spatial transcriptomics is essential to resolve the spatiotemporal architecture of intratumoral metabolism. Coupling such high-resolution metabolic maps with precise therapeutic targeting of the lactate, amino acid, and cholesterol pathways could enable rational remodeling of the secondary TME and overcome current challenges to durable immunotherapy responses.

### Secondary microenvironments disrupt antitumor networks and undermine immunotherapy efficacy

#### Loss of antitumor immunity

Although cancer therapies can swiftly remodel the immune landscape, these alterations are inherently dynamic, governed by systemic cues and sustained through complex positive and negative feedback circuits.[[150,151](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Therefore, maintaining coordinated antitumor immune networks is indispensable for achieving durable therapeutic benefits. Clinical responsiveness to ICB typically coincides with robust infiltration of effector T cells and concurrent reductions in Tregs, MDSCs, and TAMs.[[152](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Indeed, early adaptive immune activation signatures following ICB initiation strongly predict clinical outcomes.[[153](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] MDSCs represent a major barrier to sustained antitumor immunity; elevated MDSC infiltration inversely correlates with antigen-specific T cell abundance in melanoma,[[154](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] and M-MDSCs are enriched in patients exhibiting ICB resistance.[[155](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In murine models, blocking CXCR2-mediated MDSC trafficking restored responsiveness to PD-1 therapy.[[156](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Similarly, selective inhibition of phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K) γ, a kinase isoform highly expressed in myeloid cells, reinstated ICB sensitivity and promoted T cell-mediated tumor clearance without directly targeting tumor cells [[Figure 2](javascript:void(0))].[[157](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Mechanistically, PI3Kγ restrains nuclear factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells (NF-κB) activation via protein kinase B-mammalian target of rapamycin (Akt-mTOR) signaling while engaging C/EBPβ to establish an MDSC-characteristic immunosuppressive transcriptional state.[[158](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Eganelisib, a selective PI3Kγ inhibitor, has progressed to early-phase clinical evaluation.[[159](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Although macrophages are capable of tumor phagocytosis and antigen presentation, they simultaneously orchestrate ECM remodeling, immune suppression, metastasis, and ICB resistance.[[14,90,124,160](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Hence, therapeutic paradigms that limit the recruitment and M2 polarization of protumor macrophages, while enhancing their phagocytic and antigen-presenting functions, have emerged as a major focus in immuno-oncology.[[160–162](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)]

In addition to myeloid-driven immunity, secondary lymphoid reactions critically shape the quality and persistence of local antitumor responses. Recent studies demonstrated that gemcitabine plus cisplatin chemotherapy can induce a B cell-centered antitumor immune network.[[53](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Importantly, the generation of innate-like B cells, rather than terminal plasma cell differentiation, is essential for this effect. These cells amplify Th1- and follicular helper T cell-like responses through the ICOSL–ICOS axis, thereby augmenting cytotoxic T cell activity.[[53](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] This contrasts with the scenarios in certain primary tumors, where microenvironmental defects, such as the absence of NK cells, impair the capacity of plasma cell-derived antibodies to mediate effective ADCC. Instead, these antibodies promote immunosuppression through the induction of M2b-type macrophages.[[59,67,71](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Moreover, in tumors with high plasma cell infiltration, ICB-induced IFN-γ can drive β-galactoside α-2,6-sialyltransferase 1 (ST6Gal-I)-dependent sialylation of IgG antibodies, converting humoral antitumor immunity into a suppressive signal that dampens macrophage–type I interferon responses, ultimately undermining therapeutic efficacy.[[60](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Thus, both myeloid- and B-cell-dominated secondary immune responses dictate the durability and quality of post-therapeutic immunity. A deep mechanistic dissection of this cellular crosstalk is critical for designing rational combinatorial strategies to overcome immune resistance and extend the therapeutic reach of ICB.

#### Amplification of protumor inflammatory responses

Although therapy-induced inflammation is initially mobilized as a protective host response, its chronic persistence frequently evolves into a maladaptive network that fuels tumor progression, resistance, and even hyperprogression.[[7,89–91,126](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] For instance, although sorafenib-mediated anti-angiogenic therapy transiently restrains tumor growth, prolonged exposure upregulates tribbles homolog 3 (TRIB3) in tumor cells, drives neutrophil infiltration, and accelerates pulmonary metastasis in HCC.[[163](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Despite durable responses in select patients, the overall ICB response rate in HCC remains <20%,[[164](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] highlighting the profound influence of inflammatory rewiring on therapeutic outcomes. Recent studies implicate peritumoral tissue-resident memory T cells (T RM) in adverse prognosis: following ICB, macrophage-mediated antigen cross-presentation retains T RM in peritumoral regions, where their IFN-γ secretion activates NOD-like receptor family, pyrin domain containing 3 (NLRP3) inflammasomes, driving IL-1β release and angiogenesis, thereby fueling hyperprogression.[[165](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Inflammation further reshapes tumor metabolism to reinforce immune suppression. For example, macrophage-driven inflammatory signaling enhances polyamine acetylation and efflux from tumor cells, whereas acetylated polyamines reciprocally polarize macrophages toward the M2b phenotype, resulting in CCL1 secretion and Treg recruitment, thereby compromising ICB efficacy.[[166](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Thus, although therapeutic inflammation is initially antitumoral, it can paradoxically be co-opted to sustain immunosuppressive and pro-metastatic circuits.

Importantly, therapy-induced inflammation extends beyond the local tumor bed and reshapes systemic immune homeostasis. Radiotherapy and chemotherapy induce ICD accompanied by the release of DAMPs, including calreticulin, HMGB1, and ATP, which enhance DC phagocytosis and lymph node trafficking, thereby improving antigen presentation.[[7,75–78](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Yet, these same processes may facilitate metastatic dissemination; paclitaxel induces tumor cells to secrete annexin A6-containing exosomes, which are transported via circulation to the lungs, where they upregulate chemokine CCL2 and recruit lymphocyte antigen 6 complex, locus C (Ly6C)+CCR2+ inflammatory monocytes, ultimately facilitating breast cancer metastasis.[[167](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Similarly, chemotherapy-induced fibroblast senescence fosters NET formation, activates dormant disseminated tumor cells, and promotes metastatic relapse.[[112](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Moreover, prolonged exposure to immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICIs), capable of eliciting abscopal effects, may destabilize the systemic immune equilibrium, affecting cardiovascular and metabolic tissues.[[168](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Thus, cancer therapies not only remodel local immune ecosystems but also reprogram systemic immune networks through therapy-induced inflammatory cues. Future studies must adopt integrative multiscale approaches to delineate how local and systemic inflammation intersect to shape the efficacy of immunotherapy, ultimately guiding precise strategies that balance protective and pathogenic immunity.

Therapeutic Approaches and Future Directions
--------------------------------------------

Despite extensive efforts to identify robust and clinically actionable biomarkers that predict responses to ICB, results remain inconsistent across studies.[[8,16–19,47,122–125](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Stable immunotherapeutic effects depends not just on enhancing T cell infiltration and effector function but also on dismantling the immunosuppressive networks within the TME. Recent translational studies and clinical trials have explored a spectrum of combination regimens; however, a unified therapeutic paradigm is yet to emerge [[Table 1](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035#T1)]. Current strategies focus on synergistically integrating ICB with complementary interventions, including other immunotherapies, conventional cytotoxic treatments, epigenetic modulators, adoptive cell transfer, cancer vaccines, and metabolic reprogramming, to broaden patient responsiveness and overcome resistance.

### Different checkpoint combinations

Combining blocking antibodies targeting CTLA-4 and PD-1/PD-L1 improves response rates across multiple cancer types.[[169](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Preclinical studies have elucidated the overlapping and distinct effects of PD-1/PD-L1 and CTLA-4 blockade on T cells and TME, either alone or in combination.[[170](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In addition, bispecific antibodies targeting both PD-1 and CTLA-4, such as volrustomig and cadonimab, enhance T cell recruitment and activation, and increase immune infiltration, even in PD-L1 low tumors.[[170](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Likewise, PD-1 × IL-2α bispecific antibody IBI363 (Innovent Biologics, Suzhou, China) promotes selective expansion and reinvigoration of exhausted T cells in immunotherapy-refractory NSCLC.[[171](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Although combination ICB can increase immune-related adverse events, developing alternative checkpoint inhibitors may reduce their toxicity.[[172](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Novel checkpoints, including LAG-3, Siglec-15, TIM-3, TIGIT, and CD47, have shown efficacy in preclinical models, and targeting stanniocalcin-1 (STC1) or colony stimulating factor 1 (CSF1) may further enhance anti-tumor immunity.[[173](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Further clinical studies are required to determine the therapeutic efficacy and safety of targeting multiple checkpoints in patients with cancer, and to optimize strategies for developing and implementing bispecific antibodies for multitarget checkpoint combination therapy.

### Combination with microbiome manipulation

The gut microbiome has emerged as a critical determinant of antitumor immunity and therapeutic response, with dysbiosis contributing to chemoresistance, impaired antigen presentation, and reduced effector T cell priming [[Figure 3](javascript:void(0))].[[174](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In CRC, _Fusobacterium nucleatum_ promotes chemoresistance via TLR4–myeloid differentiation primary response 88 (MYD88)-mediated autophagy,[[175](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] whereas supplementation with beneficial commensals enhances PD-L1 blockade efficacy by promoting T cell infiltration and effector function.[[176,177](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Clinically, colonization by _Bifidobacterium longum_, _Collinsella aeraciens_, and _Enterococcus faecium_ correlates with superior control of metastatic melanoma,[[178](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] and fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) has produced objective responses in anti-PD-1-refractory patients.[[179,180](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Multiple ongoing clinical trials (NCT04130763, NCT03341143, NCT04951583, NCT05251389, NCT04758507, NCT04521075, and NCT05008861) are now evaluating whether microbiome modulation can overcome primary ICI resistance in melanoma, NSCLC, and renal cell carcinoma (RCC)—tumor where ICI is standard therapy but non-response rates remain substantial. Beyond melanoma, preclinical studies in CRC and pancreatic cancer demonstrate that FMT restored microbial diversity, suppressed proinflammatory signaling, enhanced regulatory immune programs, and, when sourced from long-term survivors, uniquely induced potent antitumor immunity.[[176–181](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Collectively, these findings support a tumor-type-specific rationale for ICB combination with microbiome manipulation, particularly in cancers marked by pronounced dysbiosis (CRC and pancreatic cancer), with high ICI usage but substantial primary resistance (melanoma, NSCLC, and RCC), and strong microbiota–metabolite–immune coupling. Ways to scale this approach, select the appropriate fecal donor, and maintain the new microbiome composition in the long-term, remain active research questions.

### Combination with epigenetic modulators

Epigenetic reprogramming not only drives oncogenesis, but also dictates immune plasticity within the TME, making epigenetic modulators powerful partners for ICB.[[182](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In preclinical models, low-dose histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitors reprogrammed TAMs and MDSC recruitment, restoring T cell-mediated immunity,[[183](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] whereas DNMT inhibition with decitabine enhanced effector T cell function and synergized with PD-1 blockade, markedly improving complete remission rates in Hodgkin lymphoma.[[184](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In CRC, HDAC inhibitors upregulate cytotoxic molecules in CD8+ T cells; however, their efficacy is limited when combined with PD-1 blockade alone because VEGFA-mediated abnormal angiogenesis restricts T cell infiltration.[[185](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Co-administration with anti-angiogenic therapy overcomes this barrier, highlighting a mechanistic rationale for combining ICB with epigenetic inhibitors, specifically in tumors exhibiting vascular abnormalities (such as CRC and HCC) or immune exclusion phenotypes. Therefore, ICB combined with epigenetic modulators is most applicable to tumors with epigenetically driven immunosuppression, abnormal angiogenesis, or poor T-cell infiltration; however, careful selection of dosing, timing, and patient population remains critical.

### Combination with adoptive cell therapy

Adoptive cell therapy (ACT), using chimeric antigen receptor T (CAR T) cells and tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), has achieved transformative efficacy in hematologic malignancies but faces formidable barriers in solid tumors.[[186,187](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In solid tumors, the immunosuppressive TME limits CAR T and TIL infiltration, persistence, and effector function. In this context, combining ACT with ICB has emerged as a promising strategy, where ACT provides the initial tumor infiltrate while ICB sustains its functionality.[[188](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Preclinical studies have shown that high-dose CAR-T cells eradicate tumors in mesothelioma models, whereas low-dose CAR-T cells undergo functional exhaustion, which can be rescued by PD-1 blockade.[[189](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Similarly, in a squamous cell carcinoma model, adoptive T cell transfer combined with anti-PD-L1 antibody infusion cured 60% of the animals, supporting the notion that co-administration of ICB and CAR T cells could improve the therapeutic efficacy of a single therapy.[[190](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Despite ongoing challenges, including TME-mediated immunosuppression and limited T cell trafficking, engineered CAR-T designs combined with ICB are being actively evaluated in early phase clinical trials across multiple solid tumors, including lung cancer.[[188–192](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] These combinatorial approaches hold promise for overcoming current obstacles and enhancing immunotherapy efficacy in solid malignancies.

### Combination with cancer vaccines

Cancer vaccines can elicit potent antigen-specific immune priming; however, their efficacy is frequently constrained by PD-L1-mediated adaptive resistance.[[193](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Combining cancer vaccines with ICB offers the dual benefit of providing antigenic stimulation while sustaining effector T-cell function. Preclinical studies have shown that synovial sarcoma X 2 (SSX2) cancer-testis antigen vaccine increases PD-L1 expression in tumor cells, indicating that vaccine immunogenicity can be enhanced by combining with ICB.[[194](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In clinical settings, personalized neoantigen vaccines elicit persistent multifunctional T cell responses in melanoma and glioma, with some patients remaining relapse-free for over two years and experiencing prolonged progression-free survival.[[195–198](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] In NSCLC, personalized mRNA vaccines combined with pembrolizumab significantly expanded T cell responses and remodeled the TME.[[199,200](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Similarly, oncolytic viruses, such as talimogene laherparepvec, enhance antigen release and T-cell priming, further potentiating ICB efficacy in NSCLC.[[201–204](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Accordingly, ICB combined with cancer vaccines is most applicable to tumors with high neoantigen burden and preexisting immunogenicity, such as melanoma, glioma, and select NSCLC subtypes, whereas tumors with low mutational burden or limited antigenicity may require additional or alternative combination strategies. Collectively, these findings reinforce the rationale for integrating vaccine-based immunogenic stimulation with ICB therapy to overcome primary and acquired drug resistance.

### Combination with targeting metabolic pathways

Tumor metabolic reprogramming, including lactate accumulation, aberrant fatty acid oxidation (FAO), and adenosine signaling, creates an immunosuppressive TME that impairs effector T cells while promoting Tregs and MDSCs.[[205–207](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Targeting these metabolic pathways can enhance anti-tumor immunity: inhibition of lactate metabolism alleviates T cell exhaustion and promotes tumor infiltration and cytotoxicity, whereas FAO blockade limits energy supply to Tregs and MDSCs, thereby supporting effector and memory T cell function.[[208–210](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Preclinical studies have demonstrated that PI3K inhibition reduces intratumoral Tregs and favors memory T cell formation, while CD73 blockade combined with PD-L1 inhibition increases tumor-infiltrating T cells and elevates IFN-γ and TNF-α levels.[[211,212](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Modulating glutamine metabolism and ferroptosis pathways also enhances ICB efficacy. Clinically, early studies of IDO1 inhibitors indicate restored T cell function; however, progression-free or overall survival is not improved when the inhibitors are combined with pembrolizumab, reflecting biological and pharmacological complexities rather than a lack of theoretical synergy.[[213](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] The complexities include compensatory upregulation of IDO2/tryptophan 2,3-dioxygenase 2 (TDO2) that sustains kynurenine–AHR signaling despite IDO1 inhibition[[130](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)]; incomplete intratumoral target engagement due to pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic limitations leads to inadequate pathway suppression[[130](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)]; the absence of biomarker-guided patient stratification (such as IDO1 expression, Kyn/Trp ratio, AHR signatures) dilutes potential responders; and IFN-γ-driven inflammatory feedback loops, such as activation of the cyclooxygenase-2 (COX2)–prostaglandin E2 (PGE2)–prostaglandin E receptor 4 (EP4) axis, generate secondary immunosuppressive circuits.[[214](https://doi.org/10.1097/CM9.0000000000004035)] Consequently, tumors with strong metabolic–immune coupling, such as glycolysis-driven or FAO-dependent cancers (such as melanoma, RCC, NSCLC, and CRC), may benefit from ICB in combination with metabolic interventions, whereas tumors with minimal metabolic immunosuppression may require alternative or multi-pronged strategies. Therefore, optimizing the combination dosing, timing, and predictive biomarkers is essential for successfully integrating metabolic interventions into immunotherapy frameworks. Collectively, targeting key metabolic pathways such as lactate metabolism and FAO represents a promising strategy for improving immune cell function, overcoming primary and acquired resistance, and enhancing immunotherapy efficacy.

Conclusions and Future Directions
---------------------------------

The dynamic interplay between tumor ecosystems and therapeutic interventions critically shapes clinical outcomes. However, the efficacy of ICB remains constrained by substantial interpatient heterogeneity, scarcity of reliable resistance biomarkers, and limited understanding of the spatiotemporal dynamics and hierarchical organization of the TIME. Although therapy-driven TIME reorganization provides mechanistic insights, the optimal selection and sequencing of therapeutic strategies based on these changes remains unresolved. The inherent complexity of the TIME, including diverse cellular states, spatial interactions, and adaptive feedback loops, poses challenges in identifying actionable patterns and prioritizing interventions. Integrating multidimensional data, including single-cell transcriptomics, proteomics, spatial transcriptomics, and the emerging single-cell metabolomics, offers the opportunity to dissect these complex dynamics. Artificial intelligence (AI) and deep learning can assist in uncovering the logic and temporal order of TIME remodeling, predicting patient-specific trajectories, and linking distinct immunological states with rational combinatorial strategies. Such approaches can identify metabolic, epigenetic, or functional bottlenecks driving immune dysfunction, classify TIME at high resolution, and guide the timing and selection of interventions; for example, ICB combined with anti-angiogenic therapy in vascular-abnormal TIME or with epigenetic modulators in tumors exhibiting fixed exhaustion programs is highly effective. Developing predictive and integrative TIME–treatment interaction maps that address spatiotemporal heterogeneity, biomarker scarcity, and microenvironmental complexity is a critical priority for precision immunotherapy. Continued technological and analytical innovations in these areas are expected to translate mechanistic insights into individualized therapeutic strategies, and ultimately improve patient outcomes.
