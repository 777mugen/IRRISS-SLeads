Abstract
--------

The peritumoural adipose tissue (PAT) is a key contributor to cancer therapy resistance, yet its role in regulating ferroptosis remains unclear. Here we demonstrate that PAT confers ferroptosis resistance to cancer cells by upregulating ferritin (FTH1/FTL) and sequestering intracellular iron. PAT-derived kynurenine (KYN) was identified as the principal mediator. KYN is taken up by cancer cells and metabolized to 3-hydroxykynurenine, which directly binds to nuclear receptor coactivator 4 (NCOA4). This interaction inhibits NCOA4-mediated ferritinophagy, preventing ferritin degradation and limiting the free iron pool required for ferroptosis. In murine models, pharmacological inhibition of the KYN pathway synergized with PD-1 blockade to overcome ferroptosis resistance and suppress tumour progression. These findings reveal a PAT–KYN–ferritinophagy axis that promotes ferroptosis resistance, highlighting the potential of targeting adipose–tumour cross-talk to enhance immunotherapy in PAT-associated tumours.

Main
----

The tumour microenvironment (TME) is dynamically shaped by adjacent stromal components[1](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR1 "Haynes, N. M., Chadwick, T. B. & Parker, B. S. The complexity of immune evasion mechanisms throughout the metastatic cascade. Nat. Immunol. 25, 1793–1808 (2024)."). Among these, peritumoural adipose tissue (PAT) is anatomically associated with many malignancies, including breast cancer, renal cell carcinoma and abdominal tumours such as gastric cancer (GC), colorectal cancer (CRC) and ovarian cancer[2](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR2 "Brown, K. A. & Scherer, P. E. Update on adipose tissue and cancer. Endocr. Rev. 44, 961–974 (2023)."). Clinically, PAT correlates with poor prognosis in various cancers[3](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR3 "Quail, D. F. & Dannenberg, A. J. The obese adipose tissue microenvironment in cancer development and progression. Nat. Rev. Endocrinol. 15, 139–154 (2019).") and serves as an independent predictor of recurrence-free survival in CRC[4](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR4 "Ahn, H. et al. Prognostic significance of imaging features of peritumoral adipose tissue in FDG PET/CT of patients with colorectal cancer. Eur. J. Radiol. 145, 110047 (2021)."). Adipocytes promote tumour proliferation through metabolic coupling, primarily by supplying free fatty acids and lactate to fuel tumour cell metabolism[5](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR5 "Nieman, K. M. et al. Adipocytes promote ovarian cancer metastasis and provide energy for rapid tumor growth. Nat. Med. 17, 1498–1503 (2011)."),[6](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR6 "Wei, G. et al. The thermogenic activity of adjacent adipocytes fuels the progression of ccRCC and compromises anti-tumor therapeutic efficacy. Cell Metab. 33, 2021–2039 (2021)."),[7](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR7 "Dong, K. et al. Metabolic crosstalk between thermogenic adipocyte and cancer cell: dysfunction and therapeutics. Curr. Opin. Pharmacol. 68, 102322 (2023)."). In parallel, adipocytes also upregulate CD36 in cancer cells to enhance free fatty acid uptake and secrete adipokines to facilitate invasion and migration[8](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR8 "Ladanyi, A. et al. Adipocyte-induced CD36 expression drives ovarian cancer progression and metastasis. Oncogene 37, 2285–2301 (2018)."),[9](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR9 "Di Franco, S. et al. Adipose stem cell niche reprograms the colorectal cancer stem cell metastatic machinery. Nat. Commun. 12, 5006 (2021)."). Despite these advances, the precise mechanisms by which PAT drives tumour progression and therapeutic resistance remain incompletely understood.

Ferroptosis is a regulated cell death driven by iron-dependent lipid peroxidation, making intracellular iron pivotal for its sensitivity[10](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR10 "Jiang, X., Stockwell, B. R. & Conrad, M. Ferroptosis: mechanisms, biology and role in disease. Nat. Rev. Mol. Cell Biol. 22, 266–282 (2021)."). Ferritin, composed of ferritin heavy chain (FTH1) and ferritin light chain (FTL) subunits, maintains iron homeostasis by sequestering or releasing Fe 2+ (ref. [11](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR11 "Zhang, Y. Y., Han, Y., Li, W. N., Xu, R. H. & Ju, H. Q. Tumor iron homeostasis and immune regulation. Trends Pharmacol. Sci. 45, 145–156 (2024).")). This process is linked to ferroptosis through ferritinophagy, in which nuclear receptor coactivator 4 (NCOA4) binds ferritin to degrade it and release stored iron, promoting lipid peroxidation[12](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR12 "Galy, B., Conrad, M. & Muckenthaler, M. Mechanisms controlling cellular and systemic iron homeostasis. Nat. Rev. Mol. Cell Biol. 25, 133–155 (2024)."). Previous studies revealed that activation and diffusion of lysosomal iron initiates membrane lipid oxidation and ferroptosis[13](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR13 "Cañeque, T. et al. Activation of lysosomal iron triggers ferroptosis in cancer. Nature 642, 492–500 (2025)."),[14](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR14 "Saimoto, Y. et al. Lysosomal lipid peroxidation contributes to ferroptosis induction via lysosomal membrane permeabilization. Nat. Commun. 16, 3554 (2025)."), and evidence links ferritinophagy to radiation-induced ferroptosis[15](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR15 "Zhou, H. et al. NCOA4-mediated ferritinophagy is involved in ionizing radiation-induced ferroptosis of intestinal epithelial cells. Redox Biol. 55, 102413 (2022)."), thus highlighting its critical role in regulating ferroptosis. Conversely, suppression of ferritinophagy impairs iron release and confers ferroptosis resistance[16](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR16 "Li, K. et al. TRIM7 modulates NCOA4-mediated ferritinophagy and ferroptosis in glioblastoma cells. Redox Biol. 56, 102451 (2022)."),[17](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR17 "Liu, Z. et al. E3 ubiquitin ligase DTX2 fosters ferroptosis resistance via suppressing NCOA4-mediated ferritinophagy in non-small cell lung cancer. Drug Resist. Updat. 77, 101154 (2024)."). Pharmacologically disrupting the NCOA4–FTH1 interaction reduces ferrous iron levels and ferroptosis susceptibility[18](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR18 "Fang, Y. et al. Inhibiting ferroptosis through disrupting the NCOA4–FTH1 interaction: a new mechanism of action. ACS Cent Sci 7, 980–989 (2021)."). Emerging evidence has established ferroptosis as a distinct form of immunogenic cell death that critically regulates CD8+ T cell-mediated antitumour immunity and immunotherapy[19](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR19 "Efimova, I. et al. Vaccination with early ferroptotic cancer cells induces efficient antitumor immunity. J. Immunother. Cancer 8, e001369 (2020)."),[20](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR20 "Bell, H. N., Stockwell, B. R. & Zou, W. Ironing out the role of ferroptosis in immunity. Immunity 57, 941–956 (2024)."). Our previous work also demonstrated that interleukin-1β within the TME protects tumour cells from ferroptosis by sustaining iron homeostasis[21](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR21 "Han, Y. et al. IL-1β-associated NNT acetylation orchestrates iron–sulfur cluster maintenance and cancer immunotherapy resistance. Mol. Cell 83, 1887–1902 (2023)."). However, the role of PAT in modulating ferroptosis remains unknown.

In this study, we aimed to elucidate the role of PAT in modulating ferroptosis susceptibility in cancer cells. We found that PAT elevates 3-hydroxykynurenine (3-HK) in cancer cells, which directly binds NCOA4 to suppress ferritinophagy and confer ferroptosis resistance. Furthermore, inhibiting the kynurenine (KYN) pathway synergized with anti-PD-1 therapy to achieve robust tumour eradication in adipose-rich tumour models. This study reveals a PAT-driven metabolic mechanism of ferroptosis resistance and proposes a dual-targeted strategy combining KYN pathway inhibition with immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) to enhance immunotherapy in PAT-associated tumours.

Results
-------

### PAT confers tumour ferroptosis resistance and reduces the immunotherapy efficacy

In advanced CRC and GC, tumours frequently invade through the serosa and directly contact visceral adipose tissue (Fig. [1a](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig1)). To investigate whether PAT affects ferroptosis, we established two in vivo models by implanting CRC tumours either into the mid-dorsal subcutaneous region (a non-adipose site) or into the inguinal white adipose tissue (iWAT, an adipose-associated site), as described in a previous report[22](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR22 "Iwamoto, H. et al. Cancer lipid metabolism confers antiangiogenic drug resistance. Cell Metab. 28, 104–117 (2018).") (Fig. [1b](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig1)). First, using doxycycline-inducible knockdown of the key ferroptosis suppressor glutathione peroxidase 4 (GPX4-iKD)[23](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR23 "Yang, W. S. et al. Regulation of ferroptotic cancer cell death by GPX4. Cell 156, 317–331 (2014)."), we found iWAT-implanted DLD1 xenografts were more resistant to ferroptosis than subcutaneous tumours (Fig. [1c,d](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig1) and Extended Data Fig. [1a–c](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig8)). Immunohistochemistry (IHC) confirmed lower lipid peroxidation (4-hydroxynonenal (4-HNE) and malondialdehyde (MDA)) in iWAT-associated tumours after GPX4 silencing (Fig. [1e](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig1) and Extended Data Fig. [1d](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig8)). Similar resistance occurred in MC38 tumours with inducible knockdown of the cystine transporter SLC7A11[24](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR24 "Koppula, P., Zhuang, L. & Gan, B. Cystine transporter SLC7A11/xCT in cancer: ferroptosis, nutrient dependency, and cancer therapy. Protein Cell 12, 599–620 (2021).") (Extended Data Fig. [1e–g](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig8)).

To validate the generality of PAT-mediated ferroptosis resistance, we then assessed the tumour response under pharmacological and immunological stress. In C57BL/6J mice with MC38 tumours, iWAT-implanted tumours showed markedly reduced sensitivity to the ferroptosis inducer imidazole ketone erastin (IKE), with tumour growth inhibition declining from 82.5% in subcutaneous implants to 0.24% in PAT-associated tumours (Fig. [1f,g](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig1) and Extended Data Fig. [1i](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig8)). This IKE effect was confirmed as ferroptosis dependent, as it was rescued by Liproxstatin-1 (Lip-1) in subcutaneous tumours (Extended Data Fig. [1h](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig8)). Accordingly, IHC revealed diminished lipid peroxidation in IKE-treated iWAT tumours (Fig. [1h](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig1) and Extended Data Fig. [1j](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig8)). Interestingly, PAT promoted tumour growth only in immunocompetent C57BL/6J mice, not in immunodeficient BALB/c nude mice (Fig. [1c,f](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig1)). This pro-tumour effect was abolished upon CD8+ T cell depletion (Extended Data Fig. [2a,b](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig9)). Consistently, in Rag1−/− mice, tumour growth was similar at non-adipose and iWAT sites, but adoptive transfer of CD8+ T cells selectively inhibited tumours at the non-adipose site (Extended Data Fig. [2c,d](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig9)). Thus, PAT protects tumours from both exogenous ferroptosis inducers and endogenous CD8+ T cell-mediated ferroptotic stress.

Given that CD8+ T cell-derived interferon-γ sensitizes tumour cells to ferroptosis during ICB, we additionally compared the efficacy of anti-PD-1 therapy between different implantation sites. In line with our previous observations, iWAT-implanted CRC tumours exhibited reduced response to anti-PD-1 treatment compared with subcutaneous tumours (Fig. [1i,j](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig1) and Extended Data Fig. [2e](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig9)), accompanied by diminished lipid peroxidation (Fig. [1k](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig1) and Extended Data Fig. [2f](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig9)). Consistent with the adipose-embedded status of serosa-invading tumours, lipid peroxidation signals (4-HNE/MDA) were significantly attenuated in stage III–IV tumours compared with stage I tumours that remain anatomically segregated from adipose compartments (Fig. [1l,m](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig1) and Extended Data Fig. [2g,h](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig9)) Collectively, these results demonstrate that PAT confers resistance to ferroptosis in cancer cells, thereby impairing antitumour immunity and reducing the efficacy of immune checkpoint therapy.

### PAT confers tumour ferroptosis resistance via ferritin-mediated iron sequestration

Because PAT mediates ferroptosis resistance in tumours, we speculated that PAT secretes protective factors. To test this hypothesis, we established an adipose-derived conditioned media (adipose-CM) assay for in vitro validation (Fig. [2a](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig2)). Adipose-CM significantly attenuated RAS-selective lethal 3 (RSL-3)-induced lipid peroxidation and ferroptosis in human CRC cells (Fig. [2b,c](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig2) and Extended Data Fig. [3a](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig10)) and murine MC38 cells (Extended Data Fig. [3b,c](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig10)). Although antioxidants such as vitamin E and extra-mitochondrial coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10) can suppress ferroptosis[25](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR25 "Calhoon, D. et al. Glycosaminoglycan-driven lipoprotein uptake protects tumours from ferroptosis. Nature 644, 799–808 (2025)."),[26](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR26 "Bersuker, K. et al. The CoQ oxidoreductase FSP1 acts parallel to GPX4 to inhibit ferroptosis. Nature 575, 688–692 (2019)."), their levels were unchanged in adipose-associated tumours (Extended Data Fig. [3d,e](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig10)). To systematically elucidate the mechanism by which PAT contributes to ferroptosis resistance, we conducted quantitative proteomic analysis and found that tumour cells cultured with adipose-CM exhibited distinct proteomic signatures compared with controls (Fig. [2d](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig2) and Supplementary Table [1](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#MOESM2)). Notably, both FTH1 and FTL were markedly upregulated in adipose-CM-treated cells (Fig. [2e](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig2)), while other core ferroptosis regulators remained unchanged (Extended Data Fig. [3f](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig10)). Immunoblotting confirmed selective induction of FTH1/FTL without altering other iron-related proteins (Fig. [2f](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig2)) or intracellular glutathione (GSH) levels (Extended Data Fig. [3g](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig10)), indicating a GSH-independent mechanism centred on ferritin upregulation.

Ferritin is typically upregulated by iron supplementation. However, FerroOrange staining showed reduced intracellular Fe 2+ in adipose-CM-treated cells (Fig. [2g](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig2) and Extended Data Fig. [3h](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig10)), indicating the ferritin increase was not due to iron accumulation. Dual knockdown of FTH1 and FTL attenuated this Fe 2+ depletion and abolished the ferroptosis protective effect of adipose-CM against RSL-3 in DLD1 and HCT116 cells (Fig. [2h–j](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig2) and Extended Data Fig. [3i–l](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig10)), indicating that ferritin is essential for adipose-CM-mediated cytoprotection.

To validate this in vivo, we established an mCherry-labelled MC38 tumour model and performed subsequent analyses (Extended Data Fig. [3m](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig10)). MC38 tumour cells from adipose-rich inguinal sites showed higher FTH1/FTL expression, lower intracellular Fe 2+, reduced lipid peroxidation and greater ferroptosis resistance compared with subcutaneous tumours (Fig. [2k–n](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig2)). The peritoneum is an adipose-rich environment and a common site for gastrointestinal metastasis[27](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR27 "Mukherjee, A., Bilecz, A. J. & Lengyel, E. The adipocyte microenvironment and cancer. Cancer Metastasis Rev. 41, 575–587 (2022)."),[28](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR28 "Mukherjee, A. et al. Adipocytes reprogram cancer cell metabolism by diverting glucose towards glycerol-3-phosphate thereby promoting metastasis. Nat. Metab. 5, 1563–1577 (2023)."). We therefore established a peritoneal tumour model by intraperitoneally injecting MC38 cells into C57BL/6J mice. These tumours showed higher FTH1/FTL expression than subcutaneous tumours (Fig. [2o](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig2)). Notably, FTH1/FTL knockdown inhibited peritoneal tumour progression, as indicated by decreased tumour mass and burden (Fig. [2p,q](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig2)), confirming the pathophysiological relevance of this axis in promoting peritoneal metastasis. Collectively, these results demonstrate that PAT confers ferroptosis resistance by upregulating ferritin to sequester intracellular iron.

### PAT establishes a KYN-rich TME to elevate ferritin in cancer cells

Building on evidence that PAT-derived factors induce ferritin-mediated iron sequestration, we further characterized the components responsible for this effect. The activity of adipose-CM to upregulate FTH1/FTL was heat-stable and remained after 3-kDa filtration (Fig. [3a](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig3)), pointing to low-molecular-weight metabolites as the active factor. Ferritin subunits decrease with adipose-CM dilution, confirming this induction by concentrated metabolites (Extended Data Fig. [3n](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig10)). Metabolomic profiling identified KYN as the only metabolite consistently elevated in both adipose-CM and iWAT-implanted MC38 tumours (Fig. [3b](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig3) and Supplementary Table [2](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#MOESM3)). Notably, conditioned medium from PAT of patients with CRC and MC38 tumour-bearing mice contained 124 μM and 97 μM KYN, respectively (Supplementary Table [3](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#MOESM4)). As expected, KYN dose-dependently increased FTH1/FTL, decreased intracellular Fe 2+ and suppressed RSL-3-induced lipid peroxidation and ferroptosis in cancer cells (Fig. [3c–f](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig3)).

KYN, a tryptophan (TRP) metabolite whose production is limited by indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase 1 (IDO1)/tryptophan 2,3-dioxygenase 2 (TDO2)[29](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR29 "Luke, J. J. et al. Phase 1/2 study of the indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase 1 inhibitor linrodostat mesylate combined with nivolumab or nivolumab and ipilimumab in advanced solid tumors or hematologic malignancies. Clin. Cancer Res. 31, 2134–2144 (2025)."), led us to hypothesize that PAT creates a KYN-rich microenvironment to promote ferroptosis resistance. Targeted metabolomics of tumour interstitial fluid (TIF) confirmed this, showing elevated KYN (up to 50 µM) and depleted TRP in TIF from PAT-associated tumours versus subcutaneous tumours (Fig. [3g–i](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig3)). In vitro, adipose-CM also increased intracellular KYN (Fig. [3j,k](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig3)). We next evaluated potential transporters (SLC7A5, SLC7A8, SLC7A11 and SLC1A5)[30](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR30 "Zhou, L. et al. Tumor cell-released kynurenine biases MEP differentiation into megakaryocytes in individuals with cancer by activating AhR-RUNX1. Nat. Immunol. 24, 2042–2052 (2023)."),[31](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR31 "Fiore, A. et al. Kynurenine importation by SLC7A11 propagates anti-ferroptotic signaling. Mol. Cell 82, 920–932 (2022)."),[32](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR32 "Sinclair, L. V., Neyens, D., Ramsay, G., Taylor, P. M. & Cantrell, D. A. Single cell analysis of kynurenine and system L amino acid transport in T cells. Nat. Commun. 9, 1981 (2018).") using small interfering RNA (siRNA)-mediated knockdown with KYN-FITC uptake assays in DLD1 cells. Knockdown of either SLC7A5 or SLC7A11 led to a modest reduction in KYN uptake, suggesting cooperative involvement of multiple transporters rather than one dominant pathway (Extended Data Fig. [3o,p](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig10)). Accordingly, PAT from MC38 iWAT tumours expressed higher levels of IDO1 and TDO2 than the tumour tissue itself (Fig. [3l](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig3)). Conditioned medium from PAT of mice treated with IDO1/TDO2 inhibitors (referred as adipose-CM + IDO/TDOi) failed to upregulate FTH1/FTL, an effect rescued by exogenous KYN (Fig. [3m](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig3)).

To determine whether KYN secretion is unique to PAT or adipose tissue in general, we compared PAT from MC38 tumour-bearing mice with contralateral iWAT (normal white adipose tissue (WAT)), analysing both tissue samples and their corresponding conditioned media. KYN levels were significantly higher in both PAT tissue and its conditioned medium (Fig. [3n,o](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig3)), confirming enhanced production specifically in the tumour-adjacent niche. To identify the cellular sources, we performed single-nucleus RNA sequencing (snRNA-seq) on PAT from MC38 iWAT tumours. This revealed that Ido1 was highly expressed in adipocytes, while Tdo2 was high in pre-adipocytes (pACs) and adipose-derived stem cells (ASCs) (Fig. [3p,q](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig3) and Supplementary Table [4](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#MOESM5)). Because adipocytes, pACs and ASCs collectively constitute 54.8% of PAT cellularity (36%, 14.8% and 4%, respectively), we concluded they are the primary contributors to KYN production. These findings demonstrate that PAT establishes a KYN-rich TME to drive ferritin upregulation, iron sequestration and ferroptosis resistance. Collectively, these findings demonstrate that PAT confers ferroptosis resistance to tumour cells by establishing a KYN-rich TME, which promotes iron sequestration and suppresses ferroptosis through ferritin upregulation.

### KYN-derived 3-HK is responsible for PAT-mediated tumour ferroptosis resistance

KYN is taken up by cancer cells and metabolized into downstream products[33](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR33 "Wang, W. & Zou, W. Amino acids and their transporters in T cell immunity and cancer therapy. Mol. Cell 80, 384–395 (2020).") (Extended Data Fig. [4a](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig11)). To determine whether KYN or its metabolites directly regulates ferritin, we systematically tested intermediates of the TRP–KYN pathway. Among them, KYN, 3-HK and 3-hydroxyanthranilic acid (3-HAA) were found to confer ferroptosis resistance to cancer cells (Extended Data Fig. [4b](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig11)), and notably, these are sequential products of two enzymatic steps. To further reveal their roles, we genetically inhibited these metabolic steps. In KYN-supplemented cultures, knockdown of KYN monooxygenase (KMO), which converts KYN to 3-HK, reduced FTH1/FTL, increased Fe 2+ level, enhanced lipid peroxidation and sensitized cells to ferroptosis (Fig. [4a–d](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig4) and Extended Data Fig. [4c–f](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig11)). Conversely, silencing kynureninase (KYNU), which converts 3-HK to 3-HAA, potentiated KYN’s effects, further elevating FTH1/FTL, reducing Fe 2+ and lipid peroxidation, and protecting against cell death (Fig. [4e–h](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig4) and Extended Data Fig. [4g–j](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig11)). These opposing results strongly indicate that 3-HK is the key metabolite driving ferritin accumulation.

Consistently, direct supplementation with 3-HK induced a dose-dependent upregulation of FTH1/FTL, decreasing labile iron, suppressing lipid peroxidation and enhancing ferroptosis resistance (Fig. [4i–l](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig4) and Extended Data Fig. [4k–n](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig11)). Next, we wondered by which mechanism 3-HK regulates ferritin. As neither 3-HK nor KYN affected FTH1/FTL mRNA (Extended Data Fig. [5a](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig12)), we investigated whether this is regulated by post-translational regulation. Pulse-chase assays revealed that 3-HK delayed FTH1 degradation, indicating it stabilizes ferritin by inhibiting its breakdown (Extended Data Fig. [5b](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig12)). As ferritin is degraded by NCOA4-mediated ferritinophagy[34](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR34 "Santana-Codina, N. et al. NCOA4-mediated ferritinophagy is a pancreatic cancer dependency via maintenance of iron bioavailability for iron–sulfur cluster proteins. Cancer Discov. 12, 2180–2197 (2022)."), we hypothesized that 3-HK stabilizes ferritin by inhibiting this process. Indeed, 3-HK treatment decreased lysosomal Fe 2+ in cancer cells (Fig. [4m](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig4)). Moreover, in NCOA4-knockout (KO) cells, both 3-HK and KYN failed to upregulate ferritin and lower intracellular Fe 2+ (Fig. [4n,o](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig4) and Extended Data Fig. [5c,d](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig12)). The protective effect of 3-HK against RSL-3 was also attenuated in NCOA4-KO cells compared with controls (Fig. [4p,q](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig4)), confirming that NCOA4 is essential for the function of 3-HK. Of note, pretreatment with low-dose 3-HK (2.5 μM) conferred significant ferroptosis protection in MC38 and DLD1 control cells. By contrast, in NCOA4-KO MC38 cells and FTH1‑KO DLD1 cells, low concentrations of 3‑HK failed to confer protection, and partial rescue was observed only at higher concentrations (≥20 μM) (Extended Data Fig. [5e,f](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig12)). This partial rescue at high concentrations might be attributed to the radical-trapping antioxidant (RTA) activity of 3-HK. Notably, even at high concentrations, the protective effect of 3-HK against ferroptosis remained stronger in control cells than in NCOA4-KO or FTH1-KO cells (Extended Data Fig. [5e,f](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig12)). Consistently, both KYN and 3-HK exhibited only mild RTA activity at low concentrations (Extended Data Fig. [5h](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig12)). These findings indicate that, at low concentrations, the anti‑ferroptotic effect of 3‑HK critically depends on ferritin‑mediated iron metabolism modulation, whereas at higher concentrations, the anti-ferroptotic effect of 3-HK results from a combination of ferritinophagy suppression and RTA activity.

We further examined the involvement of the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AHR), a primary target of TRP metabolites, and similarly found that AHR KO did not impair 3-HK-mediated FTH1/FTL upregulation (Extended Data Fig. [5i](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig12)), thereby excluding AHR involvement. Previous studies have reported that metabolites of KYN pathway can protect against ferroptosis by activating the NRF2/SLC7A11 antioxidant axis or through direct radical scavenging at high concentrations (50–200 μM)[31](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR31 "Fiore, A. et al. Kynurenine importation by SLC7A11 propagates anti-ferroptotic signaling. Mol. Cell 82, 920–932 (2022)."),[35](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR35 "Liu, D. et al. Tryptophan metabolism acts as a new anti-ferroptotic pathway to mediate tumor growth. Adv. Sci. 10, e2204006 (2023)."). However, we found that low-dose 3-HK (5 μM) upregulates FTH1/FTL protein levels without altering SLC7A11 expression and maintained its ability to induce FTH1/FTL even in NRF2- and SLC7A11-KO cells (Extended Data Fig. [5j–l](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig12)), suggesting a mechanism independent of the canonical antioxidant pathway. Furthermore, 3-HK-mediated upregulation of FTH1/FTL was independent of CD44-mediated iron uptake (Extended Data Fig. [5m,n](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig12)). Taken together, these findings delineate a metabolite-driven axis whereby PAT-derived KYN is converted to 3-HK within tumour cells, leading to suppression of NCOA4-mediated ferritin degradation, reduced iron bioavailability and enhanced ferroptosis resistance.

### 3-HK directly binds NCOA4 and blocks ferritinophagy initiation

To further elucidate how 3-HK modulates ferritinophagy, we investigated its impact on the interaction between NCOA4 and FTH1, an essential step initiating ferritinophagy[12](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR12 "Galy, B., Conrad, M. & Muckenthaler, M. Mechanisms controlling cellular and systemic iron homeostasis. Nat. Rev. Mol. Cell Biol. 25, 133–155 (2024)."). Immunofluorescence (IF) analysis revealed that treatment with 3-HK or KYN markedly reduced the colocalization of FTH1 and NCOA4 in tumour cells (Fig. [5a](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig5) and Extended Data Fig. [6a](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig13)). Consistently, immunoprecipitation (IP) assays demonstrated that 3-HK weakened the binding between FTH1 and NCOA4 (Fig. [5b](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig5) and Extended Data Fig. [6b](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig13)). Notably, KYNU knockdown similarly reduced the binding between FTH1 and NCOA4, whereas knockdown of KMO exerted the opposite effect (Fig. [5c](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig5)). Furthermore, we purified full-length NCOA4 (NCOA4-fl) and ferritin-binding domain (termed NCOA4 383–522) to conduct in vitro pull-down experiments[36](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR36 "Hoelzgen, F. et al. Structural basis for the intracellular regulation of ferritin degradation. Nat. Commun. 15, 3802 (2024)."). The results showed that 3-HK specifically inhibited the direct binding between FTH1 and NCOA4, whereas neither KYN nor the downstream metabolite 3-HAA exhibited this effect (Fig. [5d,e](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig5)). In addition, thermal shift assay indicated that 3-HK stabilized NCOA4 in a dose-dependent manner against thermal denaturation (Fig. [5f](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig5)), suggesting a direct physical interaction between 3-HK and NCOA4. As expected, IF analysis revealed a colocalization of biotinylated 3-HK and NCOA4 in tumour cells (Extended Data Fig. [6c](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig13)). Moreover, our streptavidin pull-down and Duolink proximity ligation assay (PLA) revealed a interaction between 3-HK and NCOA4 (Fig. [5g–i](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig5)). We further characterized the direct interaction between 3-HK and NCOA4 using surface plasmon resonance (SPR) and biolayer interferometry (BLI) with both NCOA4-fl and NCOA4 383–522. The results demonstrated that 3-HK binds directly to both NCOA4 383–522 and NCOA4-fl, with dissociation constants of approximately 0.8 μM and 0.5 μM, respectively (Fig. [5j](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig5) and Extended Data Fig. [6d–f](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig13)). By contrast, neither KYN nor 3-HAA showed detectable binding to NCOA4 (Fig. [5k,l](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig5)). Together, these findings demonstrate that 3-HK directly binds to NCOA4, disrupts its interaction with FTH1 and consequently inhibits ferritinophagy.

### Targeting KYN biosynthesis sensitizes PAT-associated tumours to ferroptosis and immunotherapy

Based on our observation upregulated IDO1/TDO2 in PAT, we hypothesized their pharmacological inhibition would reduce local KYN and, consequently, intratumoural 3-HK and ferritin. Accordingly, a treatment with IDO/TDO inhibitors (epacadostat and LM10) significantly lowered KYN in PAT and 3-HK in tumours (Extended Data Fig. [7a,b](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig14)). Immunoblotting confirmed corresponding downregulation of FTH1, FTL and NCOA4, consistent with enhanced ferritinophagy (Extended Data Fig. [7c](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig14)). Thus, inhibiting the KYN pathway in vivo effectively disrupts the ferritin accumulation axis in tumours.

We next evaluated the therapeutic potential of targeting IDO1/TDO2 to overcome ferroptosis resistance. In BALB/c mice with iWAT-implanted CT26 tumours, IDO1/TDO2 inhibitors enhanced GPX4 knockdown-induced ferroptosis (Fig. [6a,b](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig6) and Extended Data Fig. [7d](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig14)). Similar sensitization occurred in DLD1 xenografts in nude mice (Fig. [6c,d](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig6) and Extended Data Fig. [7e](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig14)). Extending to a CRC patient-derived xenograft (PDX) model adjacent to iWAT, IDO1/TDO2 inhibition also sensitized tumours to the ferroptosis inducer IKE (Fig. [6e,f](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig6) and Extended Data Fig. [7f](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig14)), and this synergistic effect was further confirmed in immunocompetent C57BL/6J mice bearing MC38 tumours (Fig. [6g,h](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig6) and Extended Data Fig. [7g](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig14)).

Because anti-PD-1 therapy promotes CD8+ T cell-mediated ferroptosis[37](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR37 "Wang, W. et al. CD8+ T cells regulate tumour ferroptosis during cancer immunotherapy. Indeed, while anti-PD-1 monotherapy showed limited efficacy against iWAT-implanted tumours, the combination of IDO1/TDO2 inhibitors with anti-PD-1 antibody further enhanced tumour suppression. Notably, this enhanced suppression was reversed by Lip-1, confirming ferroptosis involvement (Fig. [6i,j](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig6) and Extended Data Fig. [7h](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig14)). Thus, targeting IDO1/TDO2 represents a promising strategy to overcome ferroptosis resistance and enhance immunotherapy efficacy in PAT-rich cancers.

### PAT-associated ferritin regulatory axis correlates with malignancy and ICB response

To clinically validate the PAT–ferritin axis, we analysed radiographic cross-sectional imaging data from patients with GC who had peritoneal metastases. Compared with primary tumours, peritoneal lesions showed a poorer response to neoadjuvant chemo‑immunotherapy (Fig. [7a,b](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig7)). IHC analysis of paired tissue specimens in another cohort revealed significantly higher expression of FTH1 and FTL, along with reduced levels of the lipid peroxidation marker 4-HNE, in peritoneal metastases compared with primary tumour sites (Fig. [7c,d](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig7)). Furthermore, KMO high/KYNU low signature, reflecting elevated 3‑HK, correlated with advanced stage and worse survival in CRC and GC (Fig. [7e,f](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig7)). These results indicate that activation of this axis (Fig. [7g](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#Fig7)) contributes to therapy resistance in PAT‑rich cancers and may serve as a predictive biomarker for immunotherapy outcome.

Discussion
----------

Adipose tissue is abundant in the human body and distributed across multiple sites[38](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR38 "Badimon, L., Oñate, B. & Vilahur, G. Adipose-derived mesenchymal stem cells and their reparative potential in ischemic heart disease. Rev. Esp. Cardiol. Engl. Ed. 68, 599–611 (2015)."). Clinically, PAT is frequently observed surrounding malignancies including CRC, GC, renal cell carcinoma and intra-abdominal metastatic tumours[22](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR22 "Iwamoto, H. et al. Cancer lipid metabolism confers antiangiogenic drug resistance. Cell Metab. 28, 104–117 (2018)."),[27](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR27 "Mukherjee, A., Bilecz, A. J. & Lengyel, E. The adipocyte microenvironment and cancer. Cancer Metastasis Rev. 41, 575–587 (2022)."),[39](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR39 "Lengyel, E., Makowski, L., DiGiovanni, J. & Kolonin, M. G. Cancer as a matter of fat: the crosstalk between adipose tissue and tumors. Trends Cancer 4, 374–384 (2018)."), independent of obesity, which is now recognized as a state of low-grade chronic inflammation linked to metabolic disorders. Adipocytes are known to support tumours by supplying fatty acids, coupling metabolically and secreting adipokines[22](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR22 "Iwamoto, H. et al. Cancer lipid metabolism confers antiangiogenic drug resistance. Cell Metab. 28, 104–117 (2018)."),[40](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR40 "Mittenbühler, M. J. et al. Isolation of extracellular fluids reveals novel secreted bioactive proteins from muscle and fat tissues. Cell Metab. 35, 535–549 (2023)."),[41](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR41 "Zhao, C. et al. Adipocyte-derived glutathione promotes obesity-related breast cancer by regulating the SCARB2–ARF1–mTORC1 complex. Cell Metab. 37, 692–707 (2025)."). This study demonstrates that PAT suppresses tumour ferroptosis and confers immune resistance by inhibiting ferritinophagy. We identify 3‑HK as the metabolite linking KYN metabolism to iron homeostasis and show that dual inhibition of IDO1 and TDO2 restores ferroptosis sensitivity and improves checkpoint blockade efficacy in PAT‑associated tumours.

The mechanism by which PAT regulates ferroptosis has been unclear, with prior studies showing conflicting effects of adipocytes[28](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR28 "Mukherjee, A. et al. Adipocytes reprogram cancer cell metabolism by diverting glucose towards glycerol-3-phosphate thereby promoting metastasis. Nat. Metab. 5, 1563–1577 (2023)."),[42](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR42 "Xie, Y. et al. Mammary adipocytes protect triple-negative breast cancer cells from ferroptosis. J. Hematol. Oncol. 15, 72 (2022)."). Here, we find that PAT elevates L-KYN levels in the TME, leading to intracellular increase of its downstream metabolite 3-HK. Beyond known antioxidant effects, we demonstrate that 3-HK directly suppresses ferritinophagy. It upregulates ferritin and depletes labile iron independently of NRF2 or AHR[31](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR31 "Fiore, A. et al. Kynurenine importation by SLC7A11 propagates anti-ferroptotic signaling. Mol. Cell 82, 920–932 (2022)."). While 3-HK displayed only weak RTA activity at concentrations up to 50 μM (ref. [35](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR35 "Liu, D. et al. Tryptophan metabolism acts as a new anti-ferroptotic pathway to mediate tumor growth. Adv. Sci. 10, e2204006 (2023).")), we found that as little as 5 μM of 3-HK was sufficient to upregulate ferritin levels and inhibit ferroptosis. This protection was lost in FTH1- or NCOA4-deficient cells, confirming ferritinophagy inhibition as the core mechanism. Given the established role of ferritinophagy in releasing lysosomal iron to trigger ferroptosis[13](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR13 "Cañeque, T. et al. Activation of lysosomal iron triggers ferroptosis in cancer. Nature 642, 492–500 (2025)."), 3-HK-mediated inhibition of this process represents a pivotal mechanism by which PAT confers ferroptosis resistance.

Emerging evidence indicates that metabolites function not only as biological substrates but also as pivotal signalling molecules, modulating cellular processes[43](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR43 "Baker, S. A. & Rutter, J. Metabolites as signalling molecules. Nat. Rev. Mol. Cell Biol. 24, 355–374 (2023)."). For instance, fructose-1,6-bisphosphate and leucine regulate AMPK and mTORC1 signalling, the two central pathways governing metabolic homeostasis, by directly binding their regulators[44](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR44 "Zhang, C. S. et al. Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate and aldolase mediate glucose sensing by AMPK. Nature 548, 112–116 (2017)."),[45](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR45 "Chen, J. et al. SAR1B senses leucine levels to regulate mTORC1 signalling. Nature 596, 281–284 (2021)."). More recently, ω-6 linoleic acid was reported to modulate mTORC1 activity[46](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR46 "Koundouros, N. et al. Direct sensing of dietary ω-6 linoleic acid through FABP5–mTORC1 signaling. Science 387, eadm9805 (2025)."). Similarly, several TRP metabolites are recognized as ligands of the AHR[47](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR47 "Cui, W. et al. Gut microbial metabolite facilitates colorectal cancer development via ferroptosis inhibition. Nat. Cell Biol. 26, 124–137 (2024)."),[48](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR48 "Li, M. et al. Gut microbiota metabolite indole-3-acetic acid maintains intestinal epithelial homeostasis through mucin sulfation. Gut Microbes 16, 2377576 (2024)."). As an extension, our study demonstrates that 3-HK functions as an endogenous regulator of ferritinophagy and iron homeostasis. Notably, cellular iron availability influences TRP and KYN metabolism through haem biosynthesis, because haem is an essential cofactor for IDO1 and TDO2[49](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR49 "Shin, I., Wang, Y. & Liu, A. A new regime of heme-dependent aromatic oxygenase superfamily. Proc. Natl Acad. Sci. USA 118, e2106561118 (2021)."). This suggests a bidirectional regulatory circuit between iron homeostasis and TRP metabolism, potentially mediated by 3-HK.

Cancer immunotherapy has revolutionized cancer treatment. However, a considerable proportion of patients, especially those with CRC and GC with peritoneal metastases, respond poorly to ICB[50](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR50 "Kang, Y. K. et al. Nivolumab plus chemotherapy versus placebo plus chemotherapy in patients with HER2-negative, untreated, unresectable advanced or recurrent gastric or gastro-oesophageal junction cancer (ATTRACTION-4): a randomised, multicentre, double-blind, placebo-controlled, phase 3 trial. Lancet Oncol. 23, 234–247 (2022)."),[51](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR51 "Nair, K. G., Kamath, S. D., Chowattukunnel, N. & Krishnamurthi, S. S. Preoperative strategies for locally advanced colon cancer. Curr. Treat. Options Oncol. 25, 376–388 (2024)."). Our prior work showed that modulating the TME can boost immunotherapy[52](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR52 "Li, T. et al. Methionine deficiency facilitates antitumour immunity by altering m6A methylation of immune checkpoint transcripts. Gut 72, 501–511 (2023)."),[53](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR53 "Li, J. J. et al. The liver microenvironment orchestrates FGL1-mediated immune escape and progression of metastatic colorectal cancer. Nat. Commun. 14, 6690 (2023)."),[54](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR54 "Wang, F. et al. Combined anti-PD-1, HDAC inhibitor and anti-VEGF for MSS/pMMR colorectal cancer: a randomized phase 2 trial. Nat. Med. 30, 1035–1043 (2024)."). IDO1 was a promising target[55](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR55 "Kraehenbuehl, L., Weng, C. H., Eghbali, S., Wolchok, J. D. & Merghoub, T. Enhancing immunotherapy in cancer by targeting emerging immunomodulatory pathways. Nat. Rev. Clin. Oncol. 19, 37–50 (2022)."), but its inhibition combined with anti-PD-1 failed in a phase III trial[56](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR56 "Long, G. V. et al. Epacadostat plus pembrolizumab versus placebo plus pembrolizumab in patients with unresectable or metastatic melanoma (ECHO-301/KEYNOTE-252): a phase 3, randomised, double-blind study. Lancet Oncol. 20, 1083–1097 (2019)."), indicating other resistance mechanisms. Here, we found that both IDO1 and TDO2 are highly expressed in PAT, driving KYN accumulation and conferring ferroptosis resistance. Because ferroptosis is immunogenic, our findings suggest that dual inhibition of IDO1 and TDO2 may synergize with immunotherapy to improve outcomes.

In conclusion, our study uncovers a mechanism by which PAT protects tumour cells from ferroptosis and impairs immunotherapy by elevating 3-HK and suppressing ferritinophagy. These findings deepen our understanding of the mechanistic link between PAT and antitumour immunity and highlight the therapeutic potential of targeting 3-HK-mediated iron regulation.

Methods
-------

### Ethical approval

Patient samples were obtained from the Sun Yat-sen University Cancer Center (SYSUCC). The collection of these samples was conducted with written informed consent and in accordance with the regulations established by the Medical Ethics Committee of SYSUCC. The study protocol was approved by the Ethical Committee of SYSUCC (B2024-759-01). The characteristics of participants in these experiments are described in Supplementary Table [5](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#MOESM6). All animal experiments were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Sun Yat-Sen University (no. L025504202205013) and conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki. The maximum permitted tumour volume was 2,000 mm 3 or 20% of body weight, whichever was reached first. All tumours remained below this limit. Animals meeting humane endpoints were euthanized promptly, and all survivors were humanely euthanized at study completion.

### Cell culture

Cell lines, including 293T, DLD1, HCT116, A549 and CT26, were purchased from the American Type Culture Collection. The MC38 (mouse colon adenocarcinoma) cell line was purchased from iCell Bioscience. Dialysed foetal bovine serum (FBS; Thermo Fisher Scientific) was used in metabolite-treatment experiments. For routine culture, cells were maintained in RPMI-1640 or Dulbecco’s modified Eagle medium (DMEM; Gibco) supplemented with 10% FBS (Wisent) and 1% penicillin–streptomycin (Gibco), and incubated at 37 °C with 5% CO 2. All cells were negatively tested for mycoplasma contamination and authenticated on the basis of short tandem repeat polymerase chain reaction (PCR) DNA profiling before use.

### siRNA transfection and stable cell line generation

Sequences of oligonucleotides, including primers, RNA interference and CRISPR, are provided in Supplementary Table [7](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#MOESM8). siRNAs targeting human and mouse KMO and KYNU were synthesized by Tsingke Biotech (Beijing, China). For siRNA transfection experiments, Lipofectamine RNAiMAX Transfection Reagent (Invitrogen) was used according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Lentiviral particles were produced by cotransfecting HEK293T cells cultured in T25 flasks with packaging plasmid psPAX2 (2.8 μg), envelope vector pMD2.G (1.56 μg) and target plasmid (2 μg, including small guide (sg) NCOA4, shSLC7A11, shGPX4 and sgAHR) using the X-tremeGENE HP transfection reagent (Roche), following the manufacturer’s instructions. After 60 h, the supernatant containing the lentivirus was collected, centrifuged at 1,800 _g_ for 10 min at 4 °C and filtered through a 0.45-µm membrane. Target cells at approximately 70% confluency in six-well plates were transduced with the viral supernatant in the presence of polybrene (8 µg ml−1). After 24 h, the viral medium was replaced, and puromycin selection was initiated. For clonal selection, cells were plated at a density of one cell per well in 96-well plates and cultured in a medium containing 20% FBS for 10 days. Clones derived from single cells were expanded and confirmed by immunoblotting.

### Adipose-CM preparation

For preparation of human CRC peritumoural adipose-CM, freshly resected surgical specimens containing adjacent adipose tissue were promptly transferred to sterile tubes containing 5 ml of serum-free RPMI 1640 medium. The tissues were washed five times with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) to remove residual blood. The adipose tissue was carefully dissected using sterile surgical blades, followed by three additional PBS washes. Isolated adipose samples were incubated in T25 flasks containing serum-free RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 1% (v/v) penicillin–streptomycin for 24 h at 37 °C in a humidified incubator with 5% CO 2. Following incubation, the supernatants were collected, centrifuged at 400 _g_ for 10 min at 4 °C and filtered through 0.22-μm membranes to remove cellular debris and potential microbial contaminants. Control medium was prepared under identical conditions but without adipose tissue and served as a baseline reference.

For murine peritumoural adipose-CM, PAT was collected from the iWAT region of C57BL/6J mice bearing subcutaneous MC38 tumours. The procedures for tissue dissection, culture conditions and subsequent dilution for use in cell experiments were identical to those described for human adipose tissue. For subsequent cell treatment, adipose-CM or control media were diluted 1:3 with routine culture media (1640 or DMEM, each containing 10% FBS) before experimental use.

For IDO1/TDO inhibitor-treated adipose-CM, C57BL/6J mice bearing subcutaneous MC38 tumours were treated with epacadostat and LM10 for 7 days. PAT was collected from the iWAT region of corresponding mice. The procedures for tissue dissection and subsequent dilution for use in cell experiments were identical to those described for human adipose tissue, except for the supplementation of epacadostat and LM10 in the culture conditions.

### DPPH assay

The 1,1-diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl radical (DPPH) assay was conducted following the manufacturer’s instructions. In brief, DPPH was dissolved in absolute ethanol to prepare a 0.1 mM solution. KYN or 3-HK at various concentrations was then added to the DPPH solution, and the mixture was incubated at room temperature for 30 min. The reduction in absorbance at 517 nm resulting from radical scavenging was calculated relative to the PBS control. Trolox (20 μg ml−1) served as the positive control.

### Cytosol CoQ10 assay

Cytosol fractions of cells were obtained using a mitochondria isolation kit (Thermo 89874). In brief, 3 × 10 7 cells were suspended in mitochondria isolation reagent and homogenized by Dounce tissue grinder on ice, followed by centrifuging at 700 _g_ for 10 min at 4 °C. The supernatant was transferred to a new tube, and the cytosol fraction was separated by centrifuging at 12,000 _g_ for 15 min at 4 °C. Protein concentration was determined using a BCA protein assay kit. CoQ10 was detected using a CoQ10 ELISA kit (Cusabio, CSB E14081h), according to the manufacturers’ instructions.

### Quantitative proteomics analysis

Quantitative proteomics analysis was conducted by Wuhan Metware Biotechnology. In brief, MC38 cells treated with either control medium or adipose-CM were lysed using lysis buffer composed of 8 M urea, 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride and 2 mM EDTA. Cell lysates were subjected to ultrasonic disruption on ice for 5 min, followed by centrifugation at 15,000 _g_ for 10 min at 4 °C to remove insoluble debris. The resulting supernatant was collected, and protein concentrations were determined using a BCA assay kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Subsequently, the collected protein samples were used for proteolytic desalting and liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry (LC–MS/MS) analysis. Raw data-independent acquisition (DIA)-MS data were processed using DIA-NN (version 1.8.1) in library-free mode. Spectral library generation and peptide identification were performed using deep learning algorithms incorporated into DIA-NN. The UniProtKB mouse proteome database (uniprotkb_proteome_UP000000589_mouse_54910_20240528.fasta), containing 54,910 sequences, was used for target matching. The match between runs (MBR) function was enabled to construct a spectral library directly from DIA data and improve identification consistency across runs. The false discovery rate was controlled at <1% for both protein- and precursor-level identifications. Quantification was based on the remaining high-confidence identifications.

### Lipid peroxidation, ferrous iron and GSH measurement

For lipid peroxidation analysis, the cells were seeded in six-well plates one day before treatment. After exposure to the indicated compounds for the specified durations, cells were collected via trypsinization and resuspended in 200 μl of PBS containing 5 μM C11-BODIPY 581/591 (Invitrogen). Following a 30-min incubation at 37 °C, lipid peroxidation was measured using a Beckman flow cytometer with excitation at 488/510 nm, and fluorescence was detected using the FITC channel. Data were analysed using CytExpert 2.4 software.

Ferrous iron (Fe 2+) levels were quantified using an iron assay kit (Invitrogen), following the manufacturer’s protocol. Alternatively, intracellular Fe 2+ levels were assessed using FerroOrange dye (Dojindo) and visualized by confocal microscopy (LSM880 with Fast Airyscan, Carl Zeiss) or analysed using a Beckman flow cytometer. In addition, intracellular GSH content was measured using the GSH-Glo Glutathione Assay Kit (Promega), following the manufacturer’s protocol.

### Cell viability assay

Cell viability was assessed using the CellTiter 96 AQueous One Solution Cell Proliferation Assay (MTS; Promega). Cells were seeded in 96-well clear-bottom plates at a density of 2,000 cells per well in media with or without the indicated metabolites. On the following day, cells were treated with ferroptosis inducers, including RSL-3 and erastin, as well as cell death inhibitors, such as liproxstatin. After 48 h of treatment, 20 μl of MTS reagent was added to each well (containing 200 μl of medium), followed by incubation for 1 h at 37 °C in a 5% CO 2 atmosphere. Absorbance at 490 nm was measured using a Synergy Multi-Mode Microplate Reader (BioTek), and cell viability was expressed relative to the untreated control group.

### LC–MS-based metabolomics analysis

Comprehensive untargeted metabolites were analysed according to the previously described method, with modifications[52](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR52 "Li, T. et al. Methionine deficiency facilitates antitumour immunity by altering m6A methylation of immune checkpoint transcripts. Gut 72, 501–511 (2023)."),[57](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR57 "Cai, Z. R. et al. Construction of exosome non-coding RNA feature for non-invasive, early detection of gastric cancer patients by machine learning: a multi-cohort study. Gut 74, 884–893 (2025)."). In brief, samples stored at −80 °C were thawed on ice and vortexed for 10 s. Extraction of hydrophilic and hydrophobic compounds was performed using extraction solvent A (acetonitrile:methanol = 1:4, v/v) or extraction solvent B (methyl tert-butyl ether:methanol = 3:1, v/v), each containing an internal standard (IS). The sample extracts were analysed using an LC–electrospray ionization (ESI)–MS/MS system (UPLC, ExionLC AD; MS, QTRAP6500+ System). Linear ion trap (LIT) and triple quadrupole (QQQ) scans were acquired on a QQQ–LIT mass spectrometer (QTRAP), QTRAP LC–MS/MS system, equipped with an ESI Turbo IonSpray interface, operating in positive and negative ion mode and controlled by Analyst 1.6.3 software (Sciex). A specific set of multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) transitions was monitored for each period, based on the metabolites eluting within that period.

TIF was obtained through centrifugation, following the methodology outlined in a previous study[58](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR58 "Chen, S. et al. Tumor-associated macrophages are shaped by intratumoral high potassium via Kir2.1. Cell Metab. 34, 1843–1859 (2022)."). In brief, tumour-bearing mice were euthanized, and tumours were rapidly dissected within 2 min. The excised tumours were weighed, briefly rinsed with saline and blotted on filter paper. The entire preprocessing procedure was completed within approximately 2 min. The tumours were then placed onto 20-mm nylon mesh (Merck Millipore) mounted on 50-ml conical tubes and centrifuged at 106 _g_ for 10 min at 4 °C. TIF collected in the conical tubes was snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at −80 °C until further analysis. Then, 50 μl sample was extracted with 250 μl of methanol, and 10 μl IS mixed solution (250 ng ml−1) was added into the extract as IS for the quantication. The extract was vortexed for 3 min, then kept at −20 °C for 30 min, centrifuged at 12,000 revolutions min−1 for 10 min at 4 °C, and 150 μl of the supernatant was collected. The supernatant was again centrifuged at 12,000 revolutions min−1 for 5 min at 4 °C, and 100 μl of the supernatant was transferred and analysed using an LC–ESI–MS/MS system (UPLC, ExionLC AD; MS, QTRAP 6500+ System). LIT and QQQ scans were acquired on a QQQ–LIT mass spectrometer (QTRAP), QTRAP 6500 + LC–MS/MS system, equipped with an ESI Turbo IonSpray interface, operating in both positive and negative ion mode and controlled by Analyst 1.6.3 software (Sciex). TRP and its metabolites were analysed using scheduled MRM. Data acquisitions were performed using Analyst 1.6.3 software (Sciex). Multiquant 3.0.3 software (Sciex) was used to quantify all metabolites. Mass spectrometer parameters, including the declustering potentials (DP) and collision energies (CE) for individual MRM transitions, were set following further DP and CE optimization. A specific set of MRM transitions was monitored for each period, based on the metabolites eluting within that period. All metabolomics analysis were conducted by Wuhan Metware Biotechnology.

### Pulse-chase assay using HPG

HEK293T and MC38 cells at 80% confluence were washed twice with prewarmed PBS and then methionine-starved in DMEM supplemented with 10% dialysed FBS for 1 h. After starvation, 2 mM L-homopropargylglycine (L-HPG) was added to the culture medium for a 4-h pulse labelling. Cells were subsequently washed twice with prewarmed PBS and replaced with DMEM containing 10% FBS. Cells were collected at indicated timepoints (0, 2, 4, 6, 12 and 24 h) and lysed in lysis buffer (20 mM Tris, pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl and 1% Triton X-100) containing protease inhibitors on ice for 10 min, followed by centrifugation at 12,000 _g_ for 15 min at 4 °C. The supernatant was transferred to a new tube and subjected to IP using an anti-FTH1 antibody coupled with Protein A/G magnetic beads. HPG-labelled FTH1 proteins were then biotinylated via a copper-catalysed click reaction with biotin alkyne according to the manufacturer’s protocol (Click-&-Go Click Chemistry Reaction Buffer Kit). The beads were washed three times with wash buffer (25 mM Tris, pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl and 0.05% Tween-20) and boiled in SDS–PAGE sample buffer. Biotinylated FTH1 proteins were detected by western blot analysis using an HRP-conjugated anti-biotin antibody.

### In vitro pull-down assay

Purified Flag-NCOA4-fl or His-NCOA4 383–522 protein was pre-incubated with the indicated metabolites (10 μM), followed by incubation with purified ferritin overnight at 4 °C in the presence of either anti-Flag magnetic beads or Ni-NTA agarose beads. Beads were washed six times with wash buffer, and bound proteins were eluted using SDS sample loading buffer. Eluted samples were analysed by immunoblotting.

### SPR assay

The binding affinity between NCOA4 proteins and metabolites (3-HK, L-KYN or 3-HAA) was assessed using a Biacore T200 system equipped with CM7 sensor chips (GE Healthcare) at 25 °C. All measurements were performed in triplicate. Data were analysed using Biacore T200 Evaluation Software (version 2.0) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The CM7 sensor chip was activated with a mixture of 200 µM 1-ethyl-3-(3-dimethylaminopropyl) carbodiimide and 50 µM _N_-hydroxysuccinimide (NHS) at a flow rate of 10 µl min−1 for 10 min. Subsequently, 30 µl of purified NCOA4 protein (as described above), pre-adjusted to pH 4.5 by mixing with 150 µl of 10 mM sodium acetate buffer (pH 4.5), was immobilized at a flow rate of 10 µl min−1 for 5 min over three cycles. Blocking was performed using 1 M ethanolamine at 10 µl min−1 for 10 min. The reference channel was prepared with an identical activation and blocking protocol, except that PBS (pH 4.0) was injected in place of protein solution. Both flow cells were equilibrated with PBS. Stock solutions of 3-HK, L-KYN and 3-HAA (1 mM each) were serially diluted in PBS to concentrations ranging from 0 to 20 µM and injected at 30 µl min−1 for 180 s. Sensorgrams were recorded using Biacore T200 Control Software (version 2.0), and response values from the reference cell were subtracted from the experimental channel. Binding kinetics and affinity parameters (association and dissociation constants) were derived by globally fitting the data to a 1:1 Langmuir binding model.

### BLI

NCOA4 full-length (NCOA4-fl) protein was biotinylated using an EZ-Link Sulfo-NHS-LC-Biotinylation Kit (Thermo) and subsequently exchanged into PBS using Zeba Spin Desalting Columns. BLI was performed on an Octet Red 96 system (ForteBio) under standard conditions (30 °C with 1,000 rpm shaking) at a signal collection frequency of 2.0 Hz. After a 10-min equilibration, Ni-NTA and Super Streptavidin biosensors (Sartorius) were used to immobilize His-NCOA 4383–522 or biotinylated NCOA4-fl protein, respectively, onto the sensor tips. A series of 3-HK concentrations diluted in PBS was then introduced to associate with the immobilized proteins. All raw sensorgrams were baseline-aligned, and kinetic parameters were calculated by fitting the data to a 1:1 binding model. Data were processed and curves were generated using Octet Analysis Studio 11.0 software.

### Protein expression, purification and verification

The plasmid encoding His-tagged NCOA4 (pET28a-His-NCOA4 383–522) was transformed into _Escherichia coli_ BL21 (DE3) competent cells. Protein expression was induced with 0.5 mM isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside at 30 °C for 24 h. ### Thermal shift assay

The cells were collected and lysed by three cycles of freeze–thaw using liquid nitrogen and water at room temperature. Lysates were centrifuged at 15,000 _g_ for 15 min to obtain the supernatant. ### Mouse tumour models

For the non-adipose and iWAT tumour models, MC38 cells (2 × 10 6), DLD1 cells (2 × 10 6) or CT26 cells (1 × 10 6) in 100 μl of PBS were subcutaneously injected into the dorsal flank of C57BL/6J, BALB/c nude or BALB/c mice to generate the first generation of cell-derived xenograft tumours. Tumours were collected when they reached 300–400 mm 3 in volume, at which point they were minced into 1–2-mm 3 fragments. These cell-derived xenograft tumour fragments were then subcutaneously inoculated into the middle dorsal or inguinal WAT region of the indicated mice under anaesthesia. Once the tumours reached 50–100 mm 3, the mice were randomly assigned to different treatment groups for the indicated treatments. Tumour size was routinely measured using a caliper, and tumour volume was calculated using the following formula: volume (mm 3) = 0.5 × (longer diameter × shorter diameter 2).

In the in vivo ferroptosis-inducing model, DLD1 cell-derived tumours with shNC (negative control) or shGPX4 (doxycycline-inducible) were implanted into the middle dorsal or iWAT region of BALB/c nude mice. Three days after implantation, mice were randomly assigned to different groups, and doxycycline (1 mg ml−1, HY-N0565, MedChemExpress) was administered in drinking water. Similarly, MC38 cell-derived tumours were implanted in the middle dorsal or iWAT region of C57BL/6J mice. When the tumours reached 50–100 mm 3, the mice were randomly assigned to treatment groups receiving IKE (30 mg kg−1, intraperitoneally, daily, S7242, Selleckchem) or anti-PD-1 (100 μg per mouse, BE0146, BioXCell). For IKE preparation, a 60 mg ml−1 stock solution was first made in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) and then diluted in a 1:1:8 ratio of DMSO:castor oil (ethoxylated):saline to obtain the working solution. The control group received only the vehicle solution. Anti-PD-1 or IgG (100 μg per mouse) was administered intraperitoneally every 3 days for a total of three doses. Tumour growth was continuously monitored using digital calipers, and tumour volume progression was tracked over time.

For the iWAT model with IDO/TDO inhibitor administration, the TDO2 inhibitor LM10 (200 mg kg−1, p.o., HY-33298, MedChemExpress) and the IDO1 inhibitor epacadostat (300 mg kg−1, p.o., HY-15689, MedChemExpress) were suspended in 0.5% sodium carboxymethyl cellulose and administered daily via oral gavage. Lip-1 (10 mg kg−1, once daily, HY-12726, MedChemExpress) was administered intraperitoneally starting on day 0. The administration protocols for IKE or anti-PD-1 followed the same procedures as described above.

In peritoneal metastasis models, MC38 cells (5 × 10 5) were injected intraperitoneally into mice. Lip-1 (10 mg kg−1, once daily) was administered intraperitoneally starting on day 0 after cell injection. Epacadostat (300 mg kg−1, once daily) and LM10 (200 mg kg−1, once daily) were administered via oral gavage starting on day 3 after cell injection. Anti-PD-1 (100 μg per mouse) was delivered intraperitoneally every 3 days starting on day 7, for a total of three doses. Tumour burden was assessed using in vivo imaging.

### Statistics and reproducibility

No formal power analysis was performed to predetermine sample size. Our sample sizes were chosen on the basis of those used in previous studies[21](https://doi.org/articles/s41556-026-01907-x#ref-CR21 "Han, Y. et al. IL-1β-associated NNT acetylation orchestrates iron–sulfur cluster maintenance and cancer immunotherapy resistance. Mol. Cell 83, 1887–1902 (2023)."). During experiments and outcome assessment, the investigators were not blinded to group allocation. In animal studies, mice were randomly assigned to experimental groups unless stated otherwise. No data were excluded from the analyses. Data are shown as mean ± standard deviation (s.d.). Three independent biological replicates have been performed for each experiment. Numbers of experimental replicates, _P_ values and the tests can be found in the figures or figure legends. Comparisons of two groups were performed with a two-sided unpaired Student’s _t_-test or two-sided paired Student’s _t_-test. Pairwise comparisons among ≥3 independent groups were performed using one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with Tukey’s multiple-comparison test. For tumour growth curve groups, pairwise comparisons were conducted with two-way ANOVA plus Tukey’s multiple-comparison test. Survival functions were estimated using Kaplan–Meier methods, and the log-rank test was used to compare the survival distributions of difference groups. Statistical analyses were performed with GraphPad version 10.0. A _P_ value less than 0.05 indicated statistical significance. All statistical tests were two-sided. All experiments were repeated at least three times (unless otherwise specified) and yielded consistent results.
